The context
Built on Case Study 4

This project is a direct continuation of the Inverter Heat Pump Water Heater Technical Training programme. The ILT materials — slide decks, facilitator guides, and participant workbooks — developed in that engagement became the foundation for this conversion. Rather than starting from scratch, we adapted and elevated what already existed.

A global pause that changed
how the client thought
about training forever.

When travel restrictions first hit, the client's field training schedule collapsed overnight. Regional trainers couldn't reach technician cohorts; new product launches couldn't wait. The ILT programme we'd built was effective — but it was built for rooms, not screens.

By the time restrictions eased, the client had seen something unexpected: virtual delivery was cheaper, faster to reschedule, and opened the door to training technicians across Southeast Asia and the Middle East simultaneously. They didn't want to go back. They wanted the ILT rebuilt for good — for Zoom and Google Meet, the two platforms already embedded in their organisation.

  • 🌐 Technician workforce dispersed across 12 countries — classroom consolidation no longer viable
  • ⏱ Full-day ILT sessions (8 hours) had unacceptably high dropout rates when run virtually end-to-end
  • 📡 Variable internet connectivity in field locations — activities needed to be lightweight and bandwidth-tolerant
  • 🎯 Two platform environments (Zoom and Google Meet) with different toolsets — one design had to work on both
  • 🧑‍🏫 Existing facilitators trained for classroom delivery — needed upskilling to facilitate engagingly in virtual space
  • 📊 No way to track learner engagement or knowledge retention in the old ILT — client wanted data this time
The transformation

We didn't digitise the classroom. We reimagined the learning journey for a virtual environment — keeping everything that made the ILT effective, and replacing everything that made it fragile when moved online.

📋
Pre-session Pulse Survey
Sent 24 hours before each session via Google Forms. Captured prior knowledge levels, confidence gaps, and burning questions — so facilitators could customise their opening and prioritise content dynamically.
🎮
Live Knowledge Checks
Embedded at three touchpoints per session using Mentimeter and Zoom polls. Scenario-based questions replaced simple recall — learners had to apply product logic to real-world technician situations.
🔀
Breakout Room Activities
Each breakout was a structured 8–10 minute task: fault diagnosis scenarios, installation sequencing challenges, or customer role-play scripts. Task cards were pre-loaded into shared Google Docs before the session.
🧩
Interactive Digital Workbook
A fillable Google Slides deck replaced printed handouts. Learners typed notes, answered reflection prompts, and captured fault codes during the session. The completed workbook became their post-training reference.
🏆
Gamified Leaderboard
Points awarded for quiz speed-and-accuracy, breakout task quality (facilitator-scored), and post-session survey completion. A regional leaderboard was updated after each cohort — creating healthy competition between service centres.
📊
Facilitator Analytics Dashboard
A simple Google Sheets dashboard auto-populated with quiz scores, attendance, and pulse survey data. Trainers could see cohort performance at a glance and flag learners who needed follow-up before returning to the field.
What we learned
Design Decision

Splitting the day was the single biggest unlock.

Pilot testing showed that a continuous 4-hour virtual session produced engagement drop-off after the 90-minute mark. Splitting into two 2-hour sessions — with a 24-hour gap for self-study and reflection — produced measurably better retention scores and far higher facilitator ratings. The gap wasn't lost time; it was when learning consolidated.

Design Decision

One design framework, two platform outputs.

Designing for Zoom and Google Meet simultaneously required us to strip back to platform-agnostic principles. Every activity was tested for function: did it work with only screen-share and a chat box? If yes, it was safe for either platform. Mentimeter polls and Google Forms became the connective tissue that worked identically on both.

Challenge Faced

Facilitators needed a virtual presence re-boot.

Our biggest unexpected challenge wasn't content — it was facilitation. Trainers who were outstanding in the room struggled to manage virtual energy, read participant cues through a grid of thumbnails, and operate polls while presenting. We designed a Facilitator Virtual Delivery Guide and ran two practice sessions before any live cohort — a step that hadn't been needed in the ILT project.

Lesson learned

Content conversion is only half the work. Facilitator preparation for virtual delivery is equally critical — and often underestimated. We now include this as a mandatory deliverable in all vILT scopes.

Challenge Faced

Connectivity constraints reshaped the activity design.

Early pilots in Southeast Asian markets revealed that bandwidth-heavy activities — video clips, animated simulations — failed for roughly 15% of participants on mobile data. We rebuilt the session to be video-optional: all critical content was narrated over static slides, and video clips were made available as self-study materials post-session rather than embedded in the live experience. This improved reliability without sacrificing learning quality.

The outcome

Technicians logging in from 12 countries,
learning as if they were in the same room.

The converted vILT programme ran its first wave across Southeast Asia and the Middle East within ten weeks of project kick-off. Cohorts from Singapore, UAE, India, and Vietnam attended simultaneously — something the old ILT could never have achieved.

The client's regional L&D team now runs the programme independently. The facilitator guide, session templates, and activity task cards were all designed to be reused without our involvement — ensuring the investment continued to deliver long after handover.

"The virtual version is actually better. The breakouts, the live quizzes, the data we get afterwards — we can see exactly who's ready and who needs a follow-up. We never had that before."
— Head of Technical Training, Asia Pacific
