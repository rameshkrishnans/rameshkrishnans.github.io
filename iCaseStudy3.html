Case Study 3 — The DEI Programme

A half-day instructor-led training programme that moved organisations beyond surface-level DEI awareness into practical, committed daily inclusion — with role-plays, games, and personal accountability built into every module.

1
Where the story begins

DEI training that actually changes daily behaviour — not just attitudes.

The brief for this project came with an unusual level of self-awareness from the client. They had run DEI awareness training before. They could measure attitude shifts. What they could not point to was a change in how people behaved in meetings, in hiring decisions, or in everyday workplace interactions. Awareness was not the problem. Practice was.

The commission was specifically to design a programme that moved beyond awareness — that placed participants inside real inclusion challenges and required them to respond, reflect, and commit to a specific behaviour change. The programme had to work for participants who were already broadly supportive of inclusion principles and for participants who were sceptical or resistant.

The design constraint that shaped everythingThe programme had to work without alienating anyone in the room — including participants who arrived resistant or defensive. Every activity was designed to generate insight through experience rather than lecture through information. You cannot argue someone into inclusion. You can give them an experience that makes exclusion feel real.
🧠 Remember This

"The goal was not to change minds. It was to change one specific behaviour — identified by each participant, in their own context, on their own terms — and give them a structure for following through."

2
The instructional strategy

Five modules. Every concept earned through activity, not delivered through slides.

The programme was built around a single design principle: experience before explanation. No framework was introduced until participants had been placed inside a situation that made them feel the gap it addressed. By the time the DEI model was named, participants were not learning a concept — they were putting a name to something they had just lived.

1
Module 1 — The Cost of Exclusion Participants experience a simulation of exclusion — then analyse it. The stakes of non-inclusion become personal before any statistics are shown.
2
Module 2 — Recognising Bias in Practice A card game that surfaces unconscious assumptions through visible, discussable choices. Bias is not lectured — it is caught in the act.
3
Module 3 — Inclusive Communication Role-plays in pairs that practise the specific language of inclusion — how to invite, how to advocate, how to correct respectfully.
4
Module 4 — Systems and Structures Small group analysis of real workplace policies through an inclusion lens. Abstract structural change becomes concrete and actionable.
5
Module 5 — Personal Commitment Each participant identifies one specific behaviour change, the context in which they will apply it, and a colleague accountability partner.
⚠️
Challenge: managing resistance in the room

DEI training has a well-documented resistance problem. Some participants arrive defensive — feeling accused rather than invited. Poorly designed DEI programmes intensify this. Every activity had to be designed so that even resistant participants engaged without feeling targeted.

✅
Resolution

The card game in Module 2 was the key design insight. Bias is exposed through the participant’s own choices — not through being told they are biased. When someone’s own card selection reveals an assumption they did not know they held, the defensiveness dissolves. The insight is self-generated, not externally imposed. That changes the emotional dynamic entirely.

3
Making it stick

One behaviour. One partner. 48 hours.

The programme closed with a personal commitment card structure. Each participant identified one specific behaviour they would change, the situation in which they would practise it, and what success would look like. Then they named their accountability partner aloud in the room.

The public commitment mechanism was deliberate. Research on behaviour change consistently shows that social accountability dramatically increases follow-through rates. Naming your partner aloud — rather than writing a private note — activates a social commitment that private journaling does not. The 48-hour check-in window was tight enough to be real, far enough away to be after a real-world situation had occurred.

The facilitator’s role in this programmeThis programme requires a skilled facilitator who can hold the emotional complexity of the room — particularly in Module 1, where the simulation can surface genuine emotion. The Facilitator Guide included full facilitation notes, including how to handle defensive responses, how to redirect blame dynamics, and how to name tension without escalating it.
"I have been on diversity training before and I have always left feeling like I was being told I was part of the problem. This was the first time I left feeling like I was part of the solution." — Programme participant
4
What it achieved

Participants who left with one specific thing to do differently — and someone to check in with.

Q
Be ready for pushback

The tough questions. Answer them with confidence.

Interviewers will probe your decisions and test whether you genuinely own this story. Here are the objections you are most likely to face.

Approach & Objections
🤔
Why ILT? Couldn’t you deliver DEI training as self-paced eLearning for greater reach?
▾
Your response

DEI learning is fundamentally social. The most important learning in this programme happens in the room — when participants see how others respond to the same situation differently, when a peer’s card selection reveals an assumption the whole table shares, when someone names their specific commitment and has to look a colleague in the eye. None of that is replicable in self-paced eLearning.

Self-paced eLearning can deliver DEI information effectively. It cannot deliver the interpersonal experience that produces behaviour change. This programme was specifically commissioned because information-delivery had already been tried. The brief was for something different.

🤔
How do you measure behaviour change from a half-day programme?
▾
Your response

Directly, through a structured 90-day follow-up: participants completed a brief post-programme check-in at 4 weeks and 90 days, reporting on the specific behaviour they had committed to and whether they had applied it. The accountability partner structure made this self-reporting more honest — participants knew their partner could verify their account.

The honest caveat: a half-day programme cannot produce deep cultural change. What it can produce — and what this programme was designed to produce — is one specific, observable behaviour change per participant. Multiply that by the number of participants, and you have meaningful cultural movement over time.

Design Decisions
How did you handle the card game activity? What was the instructional mechanism?▾
The card game presented a set of fictional candidate profiles for a fictional job role. Participants ranked them independently, then compared rankings within their group. The profiles were designed so that unconscious preference patterns — gender, age, educational background, non-linear career paths — would surface visibly in the rankings. The debrief did not point fingers. It asked: ‘What made you rank in the order you did? What information did you weight most heavily? What would you need to be certain about before changing your ranking?’ The answers surfaced bias without accusation.
How did you adapt the programme for participants who already considered themselves inclusive?▾
The design premise was that inclusion is a practice, not an identity. Participants who considered themselves inclusive were not positioned as completed learners — they were positioned as the most important participants, because their everyday behaviour had the greatest influence on others. The commitment card was the same for everyone: one specific thing to do differently. For experienced practitioners, this was often a deeper or more nuanced behaviour — but the structure was identical.
