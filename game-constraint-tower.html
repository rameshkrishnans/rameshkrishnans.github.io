Games for Trainers

The Constraint Tower

Decision Making Team Dynamics · 9 min read

Give three teams identical materials. Give them an identical goal. Then give each team a different hidden constraint — and watch what happens. The Constraint Tower is a game that reveals more about how people work, lead, and respond to pressure than almost any other activity a trainer can run in under an hour. And the debrief, when it comes, practically runs itself.

The premise is simple. The observations it generates are not. Teams building the same tower from the same materials should produce roughly similar results — unless the invisible rules they're each working under change everything. And they do. Every time.

How the game works

Divide participants into teams of four to six. Each team receives an identical set of materials — typically index cards, tape, a ruler, and a small weight to balance on top. The goal is identical for all teams: build the tallest freestanding tower that can hold the weight for thirty seconds.

But each team also receives a sealed constraint card, read only by the team leader. The constraint is never shared with the full group during the build. It might be a budget limit on how many materials can be used. A silent rule that one team member cannot speak. A requirement to include a specific structural element. A resource that has been secretly removed from their kit. Or a time penalty triggered by any team member who touches a certain material.

"The towers they build reveal who leads, who problem-solves, who shuts down — and who finds creative solutions nobody else thought of. The debrief practically runs itself."

Teams build. The towers go up — or don't. Then comes the reveal: each team shares their hidden constraint. The room discovers that what looked like different levels of skill or effort was actually different teams operating under entirely different rules. And the conversation that follows that discovery is the real point of the game.

The hidden constraint types — and what each one reveals

The constraint you assign determines what behaviour you're surfacing. Choose based on what your workshop is trying to develop — or assign different constraints to different teams to generate a broader set of observations for the debrief.

Resource Constraint
Limited materials or budget
Reveals how teams prioritise under scarcity. Who advocates for what? Who concedes? Does the team optimise or does decision-making stall?
Communication Constraint
One member cannot speak
Surfaces how teams adapt when a colleague can't contribute in the expected way. Reveals assumptions about inclusion, and who gets overlooked when they're not vocal.
Process Constraint
Must include a specific element
Tests creativity within fixed requirements — the daily reality of most professional work. How teams respond to non-negotiable rules while still aiming for the best outcome.
Information Constraint
Only the leader knows the goal
Reveals the cost of information asymmetry and the quality of delegation. What happens when a team works without shared understanding of what success looks like?
Time Constraint
Hidden time penalty triggers
Exposes how teams manage risk under pressure. Do they avoid the risky material entirely, or take calculated gambles? Who makes that call?
Role Constraint
No assigned leader
Observes natural leadership emergence — and who gets marginalised in the process. A revealing mirror for teams who believe they are naturally collaborative.

Why it works — the psychology of pressure and performance

The Constraint Tower works because it makes abstract concepts concrete and observable. Teams don't discuss how they handle pressure — they handle it, right there, in front of each other and you. The behaviour that surfaces during the build is real, not reported. And real behaviour is far harder to rationalise away in the debrief.

The hidden constraint creates a powerful learning dynamic that most exercises miss: it means that what observers see during the build is incomplete information. Watching Team B struggle while Team A powers ahead doesn't mean Team B is less capable — it means Team B is operating under different rules. That discovery, made visible in the debrief reveal, is a direct analogue to how assumptions operate in actual working life. We judge people's performance without knowing their constraints. The game makes that problem visceral.

There is also something important about physical materials. When people are building with their hands, the usual professional composure tends to drop away. You see who takes charge, who defers, who has ideas that don't get heard, who panics when things go wrong, who stays calm and creative under pressure. These are the moments a skilled facilitator can name and explore in the debrief without anyone feeling called out — because the group witnessed it together.

How to run it — step by step
01
Prepare identical material kits
Each team gets the same items: 20 index cards, 60cm of tape, a small weight (a coin or eraser works well), and a ruler. Uniformity matters — any obvious difference between kits undermines the hidden constraint mechanic.
02
Assign and seal the constraints
Write each constraint on a card, seal it in an envelope, and hand it to the team leader before the briefing. The leader reads it privately before the build begins. Do not allow the constraint to be shared with the group — remind the leader of this explicitly.
03
Brief the full group
Tell everyone the goal: build the tallest freestanding tower that can hold the weight for 30 seconds. They have 20 minutes. There may be additional instructions their team leader will share — or not. That ambiguity is intentional. Don't answer questions about it.
04
Observe, don't intervene
Circulate during the build. Take brief notes on what you observe: who leads, who follows, who speaks up and who goes quiet, how decisions get made, where conflict emerges, how the group responds to setbacks. These observations are the raw material for your debrief.
05
Measure and reveal
Measure each tower. Celebrate the winner. Then ask each team leader to read their constraint aloud. Watch the room's reaction — the surprise, the recognition, the recalibration. This is the moment the game pivots from activity to insight.
06
Debrief with precision
Use specific observations from the build to anchor the debrief. Name what you saw — without naming individuals unless they bring it up themselves. Connect the behaviours you observed to the concepts your workshop is exploring.

The debrief questions that unlock the most

Debrief Framework
  • Q
    Before you knew the other teams' constraints, what did you assume about why they were performing differently from you?
  • Q
    How did your constraint change how your team worked together — and did everyone on the team know why things were different?
  • Q
    Where in your real work life are you operating under constraints that the people around you don't know about?
  • Q
    Where are you judging colleagues' performance without knowing what constraints they're working under?
  • Q
    What would you do differently if you ran the build again — not with different materials, but with different behaviours?

Adapting the Constraint Tower to different contexts

Workshop Adaptations

Where this game travels well

  • Leadership and management development: Assign the "information asymmetry" constraint — where only the leader knows the goal — to surface the real cost of unclear communication. The discussion that follows is almost always directly applicable to participants' actual management situations.
  • Team effectiveness workshops: Run the game with intact teams rather than mixed groups. Watching your actual team build under a constraint reveals dynamics that theoretical discussions about "collaboration" never reach. Use it early in a team development programme to establish an honest baseline.
  • Change management: Frame the constraints as analogues for real organisational constraints — budget cuts, headcount freezes, regulatory requirements. Debrief around how teams respond to constraints they didn't choose and can't remove.
  • Cross-cultural or global team programmes: The silent-participant constraint is particularly revealing in multicultural groups, where norms around speaking up, disagreeing, and deferring to authority vary significantly. Surface the differences explicitly in the debrief.
  • Innovation and creativity workshops: Use the "must include" process constraint to explore how creative teams work within non-negotiable parameters. Debrief on the difference between constraints that kill creativity and constraints that focus it.
  • Virtual delivery: Adapt the game for online teams using a shared digital canvas (Miro or MURAL) and virtual building blocks. Assign digital resource limits or communication restrictions. It's more logistically complex than in-room, but the dynamics it surfaces are arguably more relevant to distributed teams.

What to watch for as a facilitator

The most valuable observations happen in the first five minutes. This is when the team is negotiating how to work together — before the task fully absorbs their attention. Watch who sets the direction, who follows without question, and who has an idea they can't quite get heard. These early dynamics often persist for the rest of the build.

Resist explaining the constraint reveal. When teams hear each other's constraints, give the room a moment before you speak. Let participants make the connection themselves. The insight lands harder when they reach it on their own — and they usually do, within seconds.

Name what you saw, not what it means. "I noticed that in Team C, two people stopped contributing about eight minutes in" is more useful than "Team C had an engagement problem." The specific observation opens dialogue. The interpretation closes it.

The takeaway

The Constraint Tower endures as a training game because it is honest. It doesn't simulate a stylised version of workplace dynamics — it produces the real ones, right there on the table, in twenty minutes. The tower that falls over is not a metaphor. It is an actual failure, with actual causes, witnessed by the people who created it.

And that failure, explored with skill and care in the debrief, becomes one of the most efficient paths to genuine team insight available to a facilitator. Not because the game is clever — though it is — but because it creates the conditions in which people see themselves clearly, often for the first time.

The debrief practically runs itself. But only if you've been paying close enough attention during the build to ask the questions that make the room go quiet in the best possible way.
