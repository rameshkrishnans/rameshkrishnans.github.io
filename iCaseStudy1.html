1   Where the story begins

A talent market that had dried up. And a team that was leaving.

The organisation was a learning and development consultancy delivering custom training across multiple industries. Its entire business model depended on deploying skilled instructional designers to client projects. Then two things happened at once.

The external market for instructional design talent had tightened sharply. Qualified, experienced IDs were scarce, expensive to attract, and quick to receive competing offers. The pipeline of job-ready candidates the organisation had historically relied on had dried up almost overnight.

Internally, attrition had accelerated. People were leaving — not always for better pay, but for clearer career paths, better development, and the kind of structured progression that the organisation could not yet offer. Without a defined competency model, it could not credibly tell an ambitious ID what growth looked like here.

The brief came to my team in a single directive: build the internal infrastructure to hire instructional designers we cannot find in the market, train them ourselves, and develop the ones we already have.

🧠
Remember This
"The brief was not about one training programme. It was about building the function that makes all other programmes possible. That framing — designing the infrastructure, not the content — is what made this project unusual and what makes it worth telling."

The core challenge in one sentence: Build the hiring, assessment, and development infrastructure for an instructional design function — from scratch, with no existing competency standard, no formal onboarding process, and a team already under attrition pressure.

2   The competency framework

Before we could hire better, we had to define what better looked like.

The first and most foundational phase was building a competency set that reflected what instructional design actually meant in this organisation's specific context — not a generic model imported from elsewhere.

Working closely with senior stakeholders, project leads, and practising IDs across the business, we built the framework from the ground up — shaped by the organisation's own project demands, client expectations, and quality standards. The framework was structured across five domains:

  • 01
    Professional Foundations
    Communication, research literacy, professional development, data collection, and ethics — the behaviours underpinning all ID practice regardless of format.
  • 02
    Planning & Analysis
    Needs assessment, audience analysis, content analysis, and technology evaluation — the front-end rigour that determines whether a solution addresses the right problem.
  • 03
    Design & Development
    Instructional strategy, interaction design, materials development, and assessment design — the craft that turns analysis into learning experiences.
  • 04
    Evaluation & Implementation
    Formative and summative evaluation, data-driven revision, and dissemination — closing the loop between design intent and learning outcome.
  • 05
    Management & Leadership
    Project management, stakeholder management, team leadership, and business skills — competencies relevant to the Senior ID level and above.

Each competency was mapped to three seniority levels — Associate ID, Instructional Designer, and Senior ID — with clear, observable behavioural descriptors at each level. This gave the organisation, for the first time, a precise shared language for what growth in an ID career actually looked like.

🧠
Remember This
"The critical design decision was to write every competency as something observable — not an aspiration or a trait, but a specific behaviour a manager could see, a candidate could demonstrate, and a training programme could teach. That discipline is what made the framework usable rather than decorative."
3   The hiring assessment system

Replacing gut feel with structured evidence.

With the competency framework in place, we designed a structured written assessment for all ID hiring — calibrated to the seniority level of each role and anchored directly to the competency descriptors.

The assessment replaced the previous approach of unstructured interviews and inconsistent portfolio reviews. It was designed in three tiers — one for each role level:

  • 🌱
    Associate ID — Writing & Language Assessment
    Candidates were given a raw topic and asked to produce a learning objective and explanatory paragraph. No prior ID experience required — the test identified learning aptitude and communication quality, not prior knowledge of instructional design.
  • ⚙️
    Instructional Designer — Solution Design & Critique
    Candidates analysed a sample performance problem and proposed an instructional solution — format, modality, and rationale. A second task asked them to critique a poorly designed eLearning screen and suggest specific improvements.
  • 🎯
    Senior ID — Strategic Thinking & Ambiguity
    A complex multi-stakeholder brief with conflicting requirements. Candidates were assessed on strategic thinking, navigating ambiguity, and solution architecture quality — not just the mechanics of ID.
⚠️
The challenge
Hiring managers had varying levels of experience with structured assessment. There was a risk they would treat the rubric as a box-ticking exercise rather than a genuine evaluation tool.
✅
Resolution
We ran calibration sessions with all hiring managers before the first assessment cycle — walking through sample responses, discussing how the rubric applied, and establishing a shared standard. Subjectivity did not disappear, but it became visible and discussable rather than silent and unexamined.
🧠
Remember This
"An unexpected outcome was that the assessments improved the interview conversation itself. Managers who had previously relied on gut feel found that the assessment gave them something concrete to probe — turning interviews from vague impressions into focused dialogues about design thinking."
4   The training programmes

25 days from zero to deployable. And a rolling curriculum for the rest.

Two distinct training programmes addressed two distinct populations — new hires with no prior ID experience, and practising instructional designers who needed continuous development without losing project time.

For new hires: a full-day, facilitator-led ILT programme across five weeks — each week building on the last, moving from foundational theory through to applied practice and independent production. Each cohort capped at 15 participants. Four cohorts per year.

  • W1
    Foundations of instructional design
    Learning theories, design models, writing learning objectives, introduction to needs analysis
  • W2
    Audience analysis & content structuring
    Learner profiling, task and topic analysis, content mapping — participants produce a complete content map by end of week
  • W3
    eLearning design & Storyline basics
    Storyboarding, screen design principles, Articulate Storyline 360 fundamentals, interaction design
  • W4
    Assessment & evaluation design
    Bloom's taxonomy applied, MCQ and scenario-based question writing — participants produce a full assessment by end of week
  • W5
    Applied production & client readiness
    End-to-end module development, peer review, facilitator feedback, final readiness assessment

Completion of the programme was not the end. A 90-day structured mentoring period followed, pairing each new hire with a senior ID for formative review and sign-off on readiness milestones before independent client deployment.

For practising IDs: a rolling microlearning library — 15-minute self-paced modules released throughout the year, driven by a competency gap register populated from project reviews, client feedback, and manager observations. Each module followed a consistent four-part structure: concept introduction, worked example, application task, reflection prompt.

⚠️
Design challenge
There was pressure to make the five-week programme as comprehensive as possible — covering every aspect of ID theory and practice. Left unchecked, this would have produced a curriculum that was academically thorough and practically useless.
✅
Resolution
We held a strict design principle: every session had to produce a practical deliverable. Theory that could not be immediately applied to a real task was deferred or removed. By end of week two, participants had built a complete content map. By end of week four, a full assessment. The programme was always in service of the practice, not the other way around.

The organisation did not just fill its talent gap. It built a pipeline.

Within twelve months, the organisation had hired and trained 60 new instructional designers. The competency framework gave hiring managers a consistent, defensible basis for selection decisions. The structured assessment reduced mis-hires and shortened time from offer to productive deployment.

The microlearning programme shifted the culture of development within the existing ID team — from something that happened occasionally to something that happened regularly. Senior IDs reported spending less time correcting junior work and more time on the higher-complexity problems where their experience was genuinely needed.

Most significantly, the organisation was able to expand into major new markets — not because the talent market had changed, but because it had built the infrastructure to produce its own talent.

"Talent problems in specialised professional fields are rarely solved by recruiting harder. They are solved by building the internal capacity to develop the talent you cannot find."
— The project's core lesson
Q   Be ready for pushback

The tough questions. Answer them with confidence.

Interviewers will probe your decisions and test whether you genuinely own this story. Here are the objections and questions you are most likely to face.

Scope & Strategy
This feels more like an HR or L&D management project than an instructional design project. Why is it in your portfolio?
▾
Your response

Instructional design is the practice of designing learning experiences that change what people know, do, or believe. This project did exactly that — the subject matter was instructional design itself, and the audience was instructional designers. The competency framework, the hiring assessments, the 25-day training programme, the microlearning library — all of these are instructional design artefacts. They happen to address an organisational capability gap rather than a product knowledge gap, but the design process was identical.

What makes this project worth highlighting is precisely that it required applying instructional design principles at a systems level — not just building a single course, but designing the entire learning architecture that makes all other courses possible. That is a level of practice most instructional designers never operate at.

How did you decide what went into the competency framework? Was it just your judgment?
▾
Your response

No — and that was a deliberate design decision. The framework was built through structured stakeholder engagement: conversations with senior project leads about what quality ID output looked like on their projects, sessions with practising IDs at each seniority level about what skills they used most and where they felt under-prepared, and analysis of client feedback and project quality review data to identify the gaps that were actually costing the organisation.

The result was a framework that reflected the organisation's real context — not a generic ID curriculum pasted in from elsewhere. That contextual grounding was what made it credible to hiring managers and useful to the IDs who were assessed against it.

Why build your own talent instead of improving your hiring or retention strategy?
▾
Your response

We did improve the hiring strategy — that was the assessment system. And the competency framework directly addressed retention by giving IDs a visible growth path for the first time. But the market constraint was real: there simply were not enough experienced instructional designers available to hire at the volume and cost the organisation needed.

Building internal training capacity was not a compromise — it was the most sustainable long-term solution. Organisations that depend on a single talent pool to hire from will always be vulnerable to market fluctuations. Organisations that can develop their own talent are structurally more resilient. That was the strategic argument, and the outcomes validated it.

Instructional Design Decisions
25 days seems like a long time for an onboarding programme. Why not a shorter, more intensive boot camp?
▾
Your response

The 25-day structure was a deliberate design choice, not a default. The target learner was someone with no prior ID experience — someone who could write well and had the right aptitude, but had never designed a learning intervention. You cannot compress the cognitive load of learning needs analysis, content mapping, storyboarding, Storyline development, and assessment design into a week without producing someone who knows what the terms mean but cannot actually perform the tasks.

The design principle we held to was that every week had to produce a practical deliverable — not just cover material. By end of week two, participants had a real content map. By end of week four, a complete assessment. That pacing allowed genuine skill development, not just exposure. The 90-day mentoring period after the programme acknowledged that the programme was the beginning of competence, not the end.

How did the microlearning programme work alongside the formal new hire training? Did they overlap?
▾
Your response

They were designed as separate, non-overlapping systems serving distinct populations with distinct needs. The new hire programme built foundational competence from zero. The microlearning programme maintained and extended the competence of people who already had it.

A critical design decision was being explicit about what the microlearning programme was not for. It was not a replacement for the foundational programme, and it was not designed to remediate serious skill gaps. It was designed for one specific job: keeping practising professionals current and connected to a shared standard in a field that evolves quickly. Defining that boundary prevented scope creep and ensured both programmes were sharp and purposeful rather than overlapping and diluted.

What evidence do you have that the training actually worked?
▾
Your response

The most concrete evidence was business outcome evidence: 60 trained IDs deployed in year one, with the organisation expanding into new client markets it previously lacked the human capital to serve. That is a Level 4 result — the kind of outcome that justifies the investment and demonstrates that the training produced deployable capability, not just satisfied participants.

At the programme level, we tracked readiness milestone sign-off rates through the 90-day mentoring period, and monitored project quality review scores for new hire cohorts compared to self-directed onboarding in prior years. The signal was consistent: structured onboarding produced faster ramp-up and fewer quality escalations in the first six months.

Your Role & Ownership
You led a team on this. What specifically did you personally design?
▾
Your response

I owned the overall design architecture — the four-phase structure, the decision to build a competency framework before touching any hiring or training, and the principle that each programme needed a distinct and bounded purpose. I personally led the stakeholder engagement that shaped the competency framework, designed the assessment rubrics for all three tiers, and wrote the instructional design for the new hire training programme including the week-by-week structure and the deliverable-anchored session design.

The team's contribution was in content development — building out the Storyline modules for the microlearning library, running the facilitator-led sessions, and managing the logistics of the cohort programme. My role was strategic and design-led; theirs was production and delivery-focused. That division was intentional and clearly scoped from the start of the project.

What would you do differently if you ran this project again?
▾
Your response

Two things. First, I would build the evaluation framework earlier — specifically, I would define what "deployable competence" looked like in measurable terms before the first cohort started, not after. We had solid qualitative evidence from the mentoring sign-off process, but a more structured pre/post performance assessment would have given us sharper data on which programme elements were driving the most growth.

Second, I would involve hiring managers more directly in the assessment calibration process earlier. We ran calibration sessions, but they were delivered rather than co-designed. Managers who helped build the rubric had stronger buy-in and used it more consistently than those who received it as a finished tool. Participation in design produces ownership of implementation — that is a lesson I carry into every project now.
