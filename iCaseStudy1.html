Case Study 1 — The Fraud Investigation

A 30-minute branched eLearning simulation that placed learners inside a live fraud case — navigating corruption, ghost vendors, and bribery through 24+ decision nodes, where every choice steered a different version of the story.

1
Where the story begins

A fraud training that felt like homework. The client wanted something different.

The client ran annual anti-fraud training for their professional education audience. The existing programme was a click-through eLearning with policy summaries, definitions, and a multiple-choice quiz. Completion rates were high. Retention was not. The client could not point to a single moment where the training had actually changed how someone recognised or responded to fraud in their daily work.

The brief was unusual in its clarity: make the learner feel like they are inside a fraud investigation — not reading about one. Make the decisions feel real. Make the consequences visible. And do it in 30 minutes.

The core insight before design beganThis was not a knowledge problem. Every participant already knew that fraud was wrong and roughly what it looked like. The problem was recognition under ambiguity — the ability to spot fraud when it was disguised as a reasonable business situation. That required a decision-making experience, not an information experience.
🧠 Remember This

"The learner does not need to know more about fraud. They need to know how fraud feels from the inside. That is the only thing that changes behaviour."

2
The instructional strategy

24 decision nodes. Every choice a real fork in the road.

The programme was built as a branched investigation. The learner plays a newly appointed internal auditor who has been handed a routine vendor reconciliation task — that turns out to be anything but routine. As they dig into the data, three scenarios emerge: a ghost vendor scheme, a collusion arrangement between a procurement officer and an external supplier, and a potential bribery trail involving a senior manager.

The branching was designed around recognition, response, and consequence. At each decision node, the learner had to assess ambiguous information and choose an action. The choices were not obviously right or wrong — they were designed to mirror real-world situations where fraud is disguised behind plausible explanations.

1
Phase 1 — The Anomaly The learner spots a discrepancy in vendor payment records. Three plausible explanations are offered. Which one prompts investigation?
2
Phase 2 — The Investigation Armed with approval to investigate, the learner chooses how deep to go, who to interview, and what evidence to gather — each choice narrowing or expanding the case.
3
Phase 3 — The Decision Three possible endings based on the cumulative quality of the learner's investigation: case closed with evidence, partial exposure, or missed fraud entirely.
⚠️
Challenge: making wrong choices instructive, not punishing

Early testing showed that learners who hit a dead-end felt stuck and frustrated. The branching design needed to let learners experience the consequences of poor decisions without making the experience demoralising.

✅
Resolution

Every branch — including the wrong ones — was scripted to teach. A missed clue triggered a consequence that showed the learner exactly what they had overlooked and why it mattered. The learning happened in the moment of failure, not in a debrief slide after the fact.

3
The build

Built in Storyline. Every variable tracked, every path intentional.

The programme was built in Articulate Storyline 360 using a combination of variables, triggers, and conditional branching. Each decision node captured the learner's choice as a variable — which accumulated across the module to determine which of three endings the learner reached. No two learners with different decision patterns saw the same programme.

The visual design was deliberately documentary in tone — slightly grainy document images, dark interface, redacted text effects — to reinforce the investigative feel without requiring custom video production. The audio design used ambient office sounds and a voiceover narrator who spoke as the learner's internal monologue.

The production constraint that shaped the designThe 1.5-month timeline ruled out live-action video. This constraint became a creative asset — the documentary aesthetic, built entirely from illustrated assets and screen-captured documents, felt more realistic than acted video would have. Learners consistently described the experience as feeling ‘real’.

Q
Be ready for pushback

The tough questions. And how to answer them with confidence.

Interviewers will probe your decisions. They will ask why you chose one approach over another and whether you genuinely own this story. Here are the objections you are most likely to face — with responses that demonstrate real expertise.

Platform & Approach
🤔
Why branching? Couldn’t you achieve the same thing with a well-written scenario at the end of a standard module?
▾
Your response
A single scenario at the end of a module is a test. It assesses whether the learner can recall and apply what they were just taught. Branched learning is different — it is the teaching mechanism itself. The decision-making IS the instruction. By placing the learner inside choices before any explicit instruction, the programme activated the specific cognitive process that fraud recognition requires: evaluating ambiguous evidence under uncertainty. That process cannot be replicated with a post-lesson quiz, because the learner approaches it with the right answer already primed by the content they just consumed.
🤔
This sounds expensive and time-consuming. Could you have built something simpler?
▾
Your response
The 1.5-month delivery timeline and the deliberate choice to avoid live-action video kept the production cost well within a standard eLearning budget. The branching logic in Storyline, while requiring careful planning, does not require custom development. What it requires is clear instructional design — mapping the decision tree before building, not during. The investment was in thinking time, not technology cost.
Instructional Design
How did you handle the complexity of mapping 24+ decision nodes?▾
I used a decision-tree storyboard before touching Storyline — a visual map of every node, every branch, and every consequence, laid out so the entire logic was visible before any content was written. This is the most important step in branching design. If you build the map first, Storyline is simply assembly. If you try to build the map in Storyline, you create spaghetti that is nearly impossible to maintain or update.
How did you ensure the wrong choices were instructive rather than just punishing?▾
Every consequence was scripted as a teaching moment. When a learner missed a clue, the branching took them to a short narrative beat that showed what happened as a result — the fraud continued, the case was closed prematurely, a manager was not flagged. Then it offered a reflection prompt: ‘Looking back at the evidence you had, what was the signal you did not act on?’ The learning was embedded in the experience of being wrong, not in a slide that told them they were wrong.
