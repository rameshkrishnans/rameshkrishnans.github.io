Games for Trainers

The Other Side of the Table

Role Reversal Empathy · 7 min read

There is a particular kind of insight that no lecture can produce. The insight that comes from standing in someone else's shoes long enough to feel the floor shift beneath you. The Other Side of the Table is a game built around that experience. It places people in roles that are the opposite of their own — and asks them to inhabit that role long enough to discover something they didn't know they didn't understand.

It is simple to set up, takes about thirty minutes to run, and tends to produce conversations that trainers talk about for weeks afterwards. The discomfort it creates is not arbitrary. It is precisely calibrated — enough to generate genuine insight without making anyone feel exposed.

How the game works

Participants are given role cards that place them on the opposite side of a familiar workplace dynamic. The manager becomes the new hire. The experienced trainer becomes the resistant, disengaged learner. The compliance expert becomes the frontline employee who views every new regulation as another obstacle. The customer service veteran becomes the frustrated customer who has already been transferred three times.

In pairs or small groups, they then work through a short scenario — a conversation, a decision, or a problem to solve — from inside their reversed role. The scenario is designed to be recognisable: situations participants have been in many times, but only ever from one side.

"What they discover about perspective, patience, and the assumptions they carry tends to stay with them long after the room clears."

The debrief asks a simple question: what did you notice that you couldn't have noticed from your usual position? The answers are rarely what participants expected. The manager who played the new hire discovers how confusing the organisation's jargon is to someone fresh. The trainer who played the resistant learner understands, for the first time, how condescending "but this is so simple" sounds from the other side of the room.

The role pairs that generate the most insight

The game works with almost any workplace dynamic, but some pairings are particularly generative. The key is to choose roles where the power differential or knowledge gap is real and meaningful — and where each side has genuine legitimate concerns that the other tends to underestimate.

Pairing 01
Manager ↔ New Hire
Surfaces assumptions about clarity, culture, and the invisible knowledge experienced employees take for granted. Managers are often stunned by how much goes unexplained.
Pairing 02
Trainer ↔ Resistant Learner
Exposes the assumptions trainers make about motivation, relevance, and cognitive load. The resistant learner often has very good reasons for their resistance.
Pairing 03
Expert ↔ Beginner
Demonstrates the curse of knowledge — how expertise makes it genuinely difficult to remember what it was like not to know something. Invaluable for instructional designers and subject matter experts.
Pairing 04
Service Agent ↔ Frustrated Customer
Generates empathy in both directions — the customer discovers the constraints the agent works within; the agent understands why those constraints feel invisible and infuriating from the outside.

Why it works — the science of perspective-taking

Perspective-taking is one of the most studied phenomena in social psychology, and the research is consistent: people dramatically overestimate how much they understand about other people's experiences. We fill in what we don't know with what we would feel in their position — which is usually quite different from what they actually feel.

The Other Side of the Table disrupts this default by making the reversal physical and time-bounded. You're not asked to imagine what it might be like to be a resistant learner. You are one, for the next twenty minutes, working through a real scenario with real stakes in the game. That embodied experience bypasses the intellectual shortcut and forces genuine encounter with an unfamiliar viewpoint.

There is also something important about the social witnessing. Doing this in pairs or small groups means your reversal is observed — and your peers' reversals are visible to you. Shared laughter at the awkwardness is part of what creates safety. Shared surprise at what each other discovers is what creates insight.

How to run it — step by step
01
Choose your role pairs
Select role pairings that are genuinely relevant to your participants' work lives. Avoid pairs where the power differential feels punishing — the goal is insight, not discomfort for its own sake.
02
Write the role cards
Each card gives the participant their role and one specific brief: a concern they're carrying, a goal they want to achieve, and one thing they're not allowed to say directly. The constraint forces participants to communicate from inside the role rather than explaining it from outside.
03
Set the scenario
Give each pair a concrete situation to work through — a conversation, a decision point, or a problem to solve together. Keep it specific. Vague scenarios produce vague role-play. The more recognisable the situation, the more honest the engagement.
04
Run the reversal
Allow 10–12 minutes for each pair to work through the scenario in character. Resist the urge to intervene unless the conversation has completely stalled. Awkward silences are often the moment before the most interesting insight.
05
Debrief in the whole group
Ask: what surprised you about the role you played? What did you notice from this side that you don't usually see? What will you do differently in your real role as a result? These three questions, in order, produce the richest conversation.

Adapting the game to different training contexts

Workshop Adaptations

Where this game travels well

  • Management development: Pair managers with the experience of being managed — especially being managed poorly. The scenario of receiving ambiguous feedback or unclear expectations generates more empathy than any management theory module.
  • Trainer training and train-the-trainer: Placing trainers in the role of the disengaged participant is one of the most valuable experiences a facilitator can have. It rebuilds respect for the learner's position and typically reshapes how facilitators open and pace their sessions.
  • Customer experience workshops: Pair frontline staff with customer roles. Include real frustrations drawn from actual complaint data — not sanitised composites. The specificity is what makes it land.
  • Conflict resolution and mediation: Use the game as a structured pre-mediation tool. Having each party spend time in the other's role before formal discussion dramatically reduces positional bargaining and increases genuine problem-solving.
  • Cross-functional collaboration: Pair participants from different departments — sales with operations, finance with marketing. The role reversal reveals where assumptions about other teams' motivations and constraints are most inaccurate.
  • Virtual delivery: Run in breakout rooms using a shared brief document. The dynamic is slightly less spontaneous than in-room, but the insight is comparable. Consider asking pairs to share a brief video clip of their conversation with the wider group for the debrief.

What to watch for as a facilitator

Some participants will resist staying in role. The most common escape is to narrate the role rather than inhabit it — "well, if I were a new hire, I suppose I'd feel..." Stop this gently but firmly. The learning lives inside the role, not beside it.

The best debrief moments are often the quietest. When someone goes still and says "I hadn't thought about it like that" — that's the moment the game has done its job. Don't fill the silence. Let the room stay with it.

Not every pair will produce a breakthrough. Some scenarios will be resolved too quickly, some role pairs will click too easily, some participants will find the exercise artificial. That's fine. The aggregate insight across the room is what matters, and there will always be pairs whose discovery is worth sharing in the debrief.

The takeaway

The Other Side of the Table does something that is genuinely difficult to achieve in a training room: it produces empathy through experience rather than instruction. You cannot teach perspective-taking by describing it. You can only create conditions in which people encounter a perspective that isn't theirs — and discover that it's more legitimate, more complex, and more coherent than they assumed.

The participants who tend to be most transformed by this game are the ones who were most confident they already understood both sides. That confidence is worth examining. And the game does it without confrontation, without blame, and without anyone having to be wrong out loud. That's a rare and valuable thing in a training room.
