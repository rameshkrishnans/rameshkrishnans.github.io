Case Study 5 — The Competency Architecture

A competency framework and 42 unique learning pathways deployed on an LMS — reaching internal employees and external vendors through a six-modality blended learning model across an automotive manufacturing organisation.

1
Where the story begins

A workforce that needed competency architecture — not just training courses.

The automotive manufacturing client had a talent challenge that could not be solved with a course catalogue. They were expanding production capacity, onboarding a new vendor network, and simultaneously facing a skills gap across technical and operational roles. What they needed was not more training — they already had training. What they needed was a structured framework that connected training to the competencies that actually drove performance on the production floor.

The commission was to build a competency framework and an LMS architecture that could serve three distinct learner groups — internal production employees, supervisors and managers, and external vendors — through a single platform, with learning pathways that were role-specific, competency-mapped, and trackable against measurable performance standards.

The scale of the challenge42 unique roles identified. 6 learning modalities required to serve different contexts. 3 distinct learner groups with different access levels. 8 months to design, build, and deploy. And a workforce spread across two manufacturing sites and an external vendor network with limited digital literacy.
🧠 Remember This

"The instinct when facing this kind of scale is to simplify. The discipline is to simplify in the right places — and maintain complexity where it serves the learner."

2
The competency design

42 pathways. One coherent architecture behind them all.

Before any content was built, I designed the competency framework — the map of skills, knowledge, and behaviours that defined what ‘good’ looked like at every level of the organisation. This required structured workshops with department heads, production managers, and HR leadership to identify not just what people needed to know, but what people needed to do differently to make the manufacturing operation safer, faster, and more quality-consistent.

The framework was built as a three-tier model: foundational competencies shared across all roles, functional competencies specific to each role family (production, quality, logistics, maintenance), and leadership competencies for supervisory and management roles. Each competency was defined with observable behavioural indicators at three proficiency levels — developing, proficient, and advanced.

1
Foundational Competencies Safety, quality awareness, communication, and workplace standards — mandatory for all employees including vendor staff.
2
Functional Competencies Role-specific technical and operational skills, mapped to specific job families and production contexts.
3
Leadership Competencies Supervisory and management behaviours — coaching, performance management, production planning — for line leaders and above.
4
Vendor Competencies A streamlined subset of foundational and supplier-specific competencies for external vendor staff accessing the LMS.
⚠️
Challenge: getting managers to engage with the competency framework

Managers initially treated the framework as an HR exercise rather than a practical tool. There was resistance to defining observable behaviours — it felt like creating a performance management weapon rather than a development resource.

✅
Resolution

I reframed the framework as a conversation tool for managers — a shared language for development discussions rather than a performance rating instrument. By building the behavioural indicators with managers rather than presenting them to managers, ownership shifted. The people who contributed to the framework were the people who later used it most consistently.

3
The learning architecture

Six modalities. Every one chosen for a reason.

The six-modality blended model was not a design aspiration — it was a practical response to the diversity of the learner population. Production floor workers could not sit at a computer for extended periods. Vendors had limited digital access. Supervisors needed flexible, bite-sized development. Each modality was chosen because it was the right format for that specific learning context.

1
eLearning modules For foundational knowledge and compliance-heavy content — accessible asynchronously, trackable via LMS, reusable across cohorts.
2
On-the-job learning guides Structured tasks for production floor application — designed to be completed with a line manager, not a screen.
3
Video demonstrations Short technical demonstrations for equipment and process content — watchable on a tablet on the production floor.
4
Workshop sessions Facilitated group sessions for interpersonal and leadership competencies — where peer interaction was the learning mechanism.
5
Mentoring assignments Structured mentoring conversations mapped to specific competencies — with reflection guides for both mentor and mentee.
6
Assessments Competency verification activities — some automated within the LMS, some manager-observed on the floor, all mapped to specific competency indicators.
The vendor access designVendors accessed a subset of the LMS through a separate login tier with access limited to foundational and supplier-specific competencies. Their completion data was visible to both the client and their vendor organisation. This created shared accountability for vendor workforce readiness — a first for the organisation.

42 pathways deployed. A workforce with a shared language for development.

Q
Be ready for pushback

The tough questions. Answer them with confidence.

Interviewers will probe your decisions and test whether you genuinely own this story. Here are the objections you are most likely to face.

Scale & Approach
🤔
42 pathways sounds like enormous scope. How did you manage that without it becoming unmanageable?
▾
Your response

The competency framework was the management mechanism. Because every pathway was built from the same underlying competency architecture — with consistent structure, consistent modality mapping, and consistent assessment criteria — the 42 pathways were variations on a template, not 42 unique builds from scratch. The framework did the heavy lifting. The pathways were the output.

The production discipline that made this work: every pathway went through the same four-stage process: competency mapping, content identification, modality assignment, and LMS configuration. Consistency in process meant consistency in output — without requiring 42 separate design decisions for each step.

🤔
Why build a competency framework at all? Couldn’t you just identify the training needs and build the courses?
▾
Your response

Training needs analysis tells you what people do not know. Competency frameworks tell you what people need to be able to do — and at what level. The difference is the difference between building a library and building a roadmap. A library gives people access to learning. A roadmap tells them where they are, where they need to be, and how to get there.

For this client, with 42 roles and a vendor network, building courses without a framework would have produced duplication, inconsistency, and no way to measure whether the training was actually closing the performance gap. The framework was the measurement instrument. Without it, the training had no performance accountability.

Design Decisions
How did you handle the digital literacy gap with production floor workers?▾
The modality selection was the primary response. For production workers, eLearning was used only for mandatory compliance content — where the legal requirement for documented completion justified the format. The majority of technical and process development happened through on-the-job learning guides and video demonstrations, which were designed for minimal text and maximum visual instruction. The LMS interface for production workers was stripped down to the essentials: their pathway, their current module, and their completion status. No navigation complexity.
How did you build the competency indicators without them becoming a performance rating tool?▾
The indicators were written in behavioural, observable language — describing what a competent performer does, not what a poor performer fails to do. They were framed explicitly as development conversation tools — ‘where are you on this competency, and what would moving to the next level look like?’ — rather than as assessment scores. The distinction between competency development and performance management was written into the framework introduction and communicated to every manager before deployment.
