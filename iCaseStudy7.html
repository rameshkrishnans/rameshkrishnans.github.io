Case Study 7 — From Manual to Mastery

An eLearning programme combining produced video explainers, Show Me demonstrations, Try Me simulations, and scenario-based assessments — training 400 users across six role-based pathways before a high-stakes ERP system go-live.

1
Where the story begins

A go-live date that would not move. And 400 users who had never seen the application.

The organisation had completed a major ERP implementation — a new finance and operations platform replacing three legacy systems simultaneously. The go-live date was fixed. The pressure was absolute. And the training programme, which should have begun six weeks earlier, was still unbuilt.

The challenge was not just speed. Different user groups needed different training — finance teams, operations managers, and front-line processing staff each had distinct workflows within the same system. A single generic programme would not work. Six bespoke role-based pathways had to be designed, built, and deployed in under four months.

The content audit finding that changed the designSME interviews revealed that the most common errors users were expected to make were not about understanding the system — they were about understanding the underlying business process the system was designed to support. This shifted the entire instructional strategy: teach the process first, then the system. Every video, every Show Me, every Try Me was sequenced accordingly.
🧠 Remember This

"The system is the tool. The business process is the task. If users do not understand the task, no amount of system training will produce the right behaviour."

2
The four-layer design

Explain, demonstrate, practise, assess. Every layer building on the one before.

The programme was built as a four-layer learning architecture applied consistently across all six role pathways.

1
Video Explainers (42 videos, avg. 3.8 min) Produced animations with professional voiceover. Each video established the business context before introducing the system interface. Deliberately non-technical in tone — designed to be watched, not studied.
2
Show Me (guided demonstrations) Step-by-step guided walkthroughs of the actual system interface in Storyline — with hotspots, callout annotations, and audio commentary. Every click annotated with purpose, not just instruction.
3
Try Me (guided practice) Pixel-accurate system simulation where users completed real tasks independently. Three levels of support: full hints, hints on request, and mastery mode (no hints). Errors triggered specific feedback, not generic ‘incorrect’ messages.
4
Scenario-Based Assessment Not a knowledge quiz — a realistic work situation requiring the right decision within the system. Results fed a live dashboard visible to learning administrators and line managers.
⚠️
Challenge: the system kept changing

Screen captures taken during early development became obsolete as the vendor made interface changes right up to four weeks before go-live. Show Me demonstrations built against earlier builds had to be rebuilt from scratch.

✅
Resolution

I introduced a screen freeze policy — no interface changes after week ten without a formal impact assessment. I also built a rapid-rebuild workflow that could update a Show Me within 48 hours of a confirmed screen change.

3
What made this hard

Six challenges. Six resolutions.

⚠️
SME availability

The people who held all the knowledge were also implementing the system. Long review cycles threatened the build phase.

✅
Resolution

Targeted sign-off model: each SME assigned to specific modules only, with a 48-hour turnaround expectation and a structured review template focused on factual accuracy — not editorial preference.

⚠️
Role overlap

Many screens were shared across multiple user roles but used differently by each. Building separate sequences for every role would have tripled build time.

✅
Resolution

Layered branching within shared modules — a single core Show Me with role-specific annotation layers that activated based on the user’s pathway.

⚠️
Engagement: system training as box-ticking

Early pilot feedback confirmed participants were clicking through Show Me sequences without reading the annotations.

✅
Resolution

Consequence moments embedded before every Show Me — brief scenarios showing what happens when a common error is made in the real system. Users who understood the cost of getting it wrong paid far more attention to the demonstration.

⚠️
Assessment integrity

Early assessment designs were flagged as too easy — users could pass by eliminating obviously wrong options without genuine understanding.

✅
Resolution

All knowledge-recall questions replaced with scenario-based situational questions. Distractors written to be plausible — close-but-wrong options that would catch vague rather than solid understanding.

Q
Be ready for pushback

The tough questions. Answer them with confidence.

Interviewers will probe your decisions and test whether you genuinely own this story. Here are the objections you are most likely to face.

Design Approach
🤔
Why 42 produced videos? Couldn’t you use screen recordings instead? They would be faster and cheaper.
▾
Your response

Screen recordings show what the system does. Produced videos explain why the system does what it does — and why the underlying business process demands it. A screen recording of a finance approval workflow shows the clicks. A produced video explains what an approval actually authorises, what happens if it is skipped, and how it connects to the payment run. That context is what makes the subsequent Show Me meaningful rather than mechanical.

The speed argument also underestimates the production system we built: a modular animation component library, a pre-approved script template, and a voiceover artist on retainer. A video could go from first-draft script to finished output in five working days. That pace made 42 videos viable within a four-month window.

🤔
Why not one programme for all users rather than six role-based pathways?
▾
Your response

A generic programme would have trained every user in every workflow — including workflows irrelevant to their role. In a system where data entry errors have financial consequences, training people in processes they will never use creates confusion, not competence. Worse, it wastes the single most valuable resource in a pre-go-live window: learner attention.

The role-based pathways were also more efficient than they appear: many modules were shared across roles, with role-specific annotation layers applied to the same core content. The 18 modules served six pathways — meaning the average module was used by multiple roles, not built exclusively for one.

Show Me / Try Me Design
What is the instructional difference between a Show Me and a Try Me?▾
A Show Me is a guided demonstration — the learner watches, reads, and listens, but does not interact. It builds a mental model of the workflow. A Try Me is a guided practice — the learner completes the same task independently, with support available at three levels depending on their confidence. The Show Me builds the schema. The Try Me tests and consolidates it. Neither is effective without the other. A Show Me without a Try Me produces recognition without production. A Try Me without a Show Me produces trial-and-error without a mental model to anchor the learning.
Why scenario-based assessment rather than a system simulation for the summative?▾
A system simulation tests whether a user can navigate the interface. A scenario-based assessment tests whether a user makes the right business decision — which is the actual performance requirement. A finance user who can click through a payment run correctly but does not know when to escalate an approval exception has passed the interface test and failed the job performance test. The scenario assessment targeted the judgment layer that the Try Me activity, focused on procedural practice, could not reach.
