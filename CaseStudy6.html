The challenge

An infection-control module
that no one took
seriously.

For a large private hospital network with fourteen sites and over three thousand clinical staff, infection prevention and control is not optional — it is a regulatory mandate and a patient safety imperative. Every nurse, healthcare assistant, and allied health professional was required to complete an annual IPC assessment before their practice certificate was renewed.

The problem was well-known inside the L&D team and quietly acknowledged by clinical leads: the existing assessment was being completed, but not engaged with. Staff moved through it mechanically — not because they were careless, but because the assessment gave them no reason to care. A series of text-heavy scenario questions with no consequence for wrong answers and no connection to the actual wards they worked on every day had trained people to click through, not to think.

The brief was direct: redesign the assessment experience so that clinical staff genuinely process the questions — and so that the knowledge they demonstrate in the assessment holds in their hands when it matters on the ward.

The concept

What if the assessment didn't test clinical knowledge about patients —
but put you in charge of one?

The ward round brief

Learners are introduced to a patient — Mrs Adaeze Okoro, 68, post-operative day two following a hip replacement. Over the course of six clinical decision points — one in each department she passes through on her path to discharge — learners are the clinician responsible for her care. Every question is drawn directly from the IPC protocols governing that specific ward. Every wrong decision costs Care Credits. Arrive at discharge with 500 credits intact and Mrs Okoro goes home healthy. Lose too many credits and the simulation flags a clinical incident — and every learner who triggers one replays the ward round voluntarily, without being asked.

Mechanic 01
The Care Credit system
Every learner begins with 500 Care Credits — a visible, persistent balance displayed throughout the experience. Correct clinical decisions preserve the balance. Each error costs 100 credits, with a brief explanation of the real-world consequence that decision would have had for Mrs Okoro.
Mechanic 02
Contextualised ward questions
Questions are written for the specific department the patient is in at each stage — A&E triage protocols in the emergency bay, surgical site infection indicators in the post-op suite, hand hygiene decision trees in the rehabilitation ward. No generic scenarios. No transferable click-patterns.
Mechanic 03
The incident report reveal
Learners who deplete their credit balance do not simply fail — they receive a simulated clinical incident report bearing their name and their decisions. The design is deliberately uncomfortable. It is also, by unanimous feedback from the first cohort, the most memorable piece of IPC training they had ever encountered.
The mechanic

A balance that
reflects the weight
of every decision.

The Care Credit system was designed around a simple behavioural insight: people protect what they can see they are losing. The existing assessment had no visible stake — a correct answer and an incorrect answer looked identical from the learner's perspective. The score appeared only at the end, by which time the opportunity to change behaviour had passed.

By surfacing the credit balance persistently — updated in real time after every question, with a brief animated loss sequence for errors — learners experienced each decision as consequential. Not abstractly consequential in a real clinical situation they might encounter, but immediately consequential in a simulation they were living through at that moment.

The credit threshold was calibrated carefully. One error was survivable. Two made a perfect-score outcome impossible but left the patient — and the learner's professional standing in the simulation — intact. Three errors triggered the incident report. The design rewarded careful thinking without being punishing enough to feel unfair.

Design principle The 100-credit penalty was not chosen arbitrarily. It maps to a real consequence weighting used in the hospital's IPC audit framework — errors in hand hygiene, PPE selection, and isolation protocol are classified as high-risk events. The simulation's stakes mirror the real ones.

The design challenge
Challenge one

Making the patient feel real

For the Care Credit system to work, Mrs Okoro had to feel like a person, not a case study prop. The design team spent as much time on her characterisation — her family, her concerns about her hip, the small details of her post-operative progress — as on the clinical content itself. The hypothesis was that learners who felt responsible for a specific individual would engage differently than those answering questions about a generic "patient."

The hypothesis held. Debrief interviews revealed that learners consistently referred to Mrs Okoro by name — not as "the patient" — and that several spontaneously mentioned not wanting to let her down as a factor in their level of care with each question.

Design decision A short non-skippable introduction sequence — ninety seconds — established Mrs Okoro's story, her family, and what a successful discharge would mean for her before the first clinical question appeared. Learners were invested before the stakes were introduced.
Challenge two

The incident report as a learning tool

The most debated element of the design was the clinical incident report triggered by three or more errors. The clinical governance team was initially concerned that it would feel punitive rather than educational. The compromise reached was precise: the incident report would include not just the errors, but a clear clinical narrative explaining how each error could have harmed Mrs Okoro — and what the correct protocol would have achieved instead.

The result was a document that felt simultaneously serious and entirely constructive. It did not blame. It explained — with specificity — the gap between what the learner knew intellectually and what their decisions had revealed in practice.

Outcome Every learner who received an incident report on first attempt completed the ward round again within 24 hours — unprompted. The voluntary replay rate for incident-report recipients was 100%.

The outcome

Staff stopped ticking boxes.
They started protecting patients.

The response from the first cohort of four hundred clinical staff was swift and unequivocal. Average time-on-assessment rose from under seven minutes to just over nineteen — not because the module was longer, but because learners were reading carefully, deliberating, and replaying sequences to understand their errors. The ward round was being experienced, not processed.

The L&D team had anticipated resistance to the credit-loss mechanic — particularly from experienced senior staff who might feel the simulation was beneath their clinical expertise. What they found instead was that experienced staff engaged most intensely. They knew the stakes. The simulation mirrored real consequences they understood from their own practice, and being made to face their decision-making in a structured way was something many reported as genuinely valuable rather than merely procedural.

IPC compliance audits at eight weeks post-training — the point at which previous cohorts had historically shown a marked decline — showed no such drop. The behaviour change extended further than any previous training model had achieved.
