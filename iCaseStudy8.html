Case Study 8 — The Learning That Never Stops

An eLearning programme for frontline humanitarian workers that used Degreed LXP to bring live, AI-curated articles, reports, and videos from across the internet into the course — keeping knowledge current long after the final module was complete.

1
Where the story begins

A field that changed daily. A course that updated annually.

The organisation deployed frontline staff across conflict zones, refugee displacement corridors, and communities affected by exploitation and trafficking. Their work demanded not just foundational knowledge — of child protection protocols, trauma-informed practice, and safeguarding policy — but a continuous, real-time understanding of a world that changed daily.

The existing eLearning was a set of fixed modules, updated annually at best. By the time a new staff member completed it, landmark reports had been published, practice guidance had evolved, and the crisis situations the training referenced had moved on. The course was always, to some degree, already out of date.

The design problem this createdA fixed course creates a false sense of completeness. Learners who finish it feel they have ‘done’ their learning. In a field where a major report or a significant crisis development can change practice guidance within days, that sense of completeness is the most dangerous thing a training programme can produce.
🧠 Remember This

"The goal was not a better course. It was a different relationship between the learner and learning — one that did not end when the modules were complete, because the world did not stop either."

2
The two-layer approach

Storyline for the foundations. Degreed for everything the foundations couldn’t contain.

The solution was a two-layer learning architecture. Six Storyline modules delivered the foundational knowledge every staff member needed — the frameworks, the policy requirements, the critical decision-making tools. Degreed LXP delivered the live, evolving, AI-curated knowledge of the field as it actually existed right now.

1
Storyline core modules 6 modules covering safeguarding, trauma-informed practice, community engagement, staff safety, reporting, and context — each ending with a Degreed content feed embedded via web object.
2
Degreed AI curation AI engine scanning the web for relevant articles, NGO reports, video talks, and podcasts — calibrated to each learner’s role, region, and engagement history. Updated daily.
3
xAPI integration Storyline completions and Degreed engagement tracked in a unified Learning Record Store — giving a single view of mandatory compliance and voluntary knowledge-building.
4
Embedded web objects Selected Degreed content embedded directly inside Storyline modules at the exact instructional moment where it added most value — showing a live feed inside the course.
⚠️
Challenge: content sensitivity

Not all internet content on conflict, trafficking, or exploitation was appropriate or safe to serve to field staff without curation guardrails. The AI needed boundaries.

✅
Resolution

The Degreed AI engine was configured with a trusted source taxonomy — seeded with approved domains including UN agency publications, major humanitarian journals, established NGO research outputs, and reputable international journalism. Sources outside the taxonomy required manual review before appearing in the feed.

3
What the course covered

Six modules that taught foundations — and pointed outward.

The Storyline modules were deliberately scoped to do less than a conventional eLearning programme would attempt. Each module focused on the foundational frameworks a staff member could not function without — then explicitly named what it could not cover and directed learners outward into Degreed to find it.

This was a philosophy as much as a design decision. The course stopped trying to be the source of all knowledge. It became the lens through which learners could engage with a much richer world of knowledge beyond it.

1
Module 1 — Understanding the Context The global landscape of conflict, displacement, and exploitation — grounding all subsequent learning in field reality, not abstraction.
2
Module 2 — Safeguarding Principles Core frameworks, mandatory reporting obligations, and branched scenarios placing staff inside situations where they had to apply them.
3
Module 3 — Trauma-Informed Practice How trauma shapes behaviour and communication — and how staff can engage in ways that support recovery.
4
Module 4 — Community Engagement Cultural competence, trust with local partners, avoiding the imposition of external assumptions.
5
Module 5 — Staff Safety & Resilience Security protocols, field risk assessment, vicarious trauma recognition and support.
6
Module 6 — Reporting & Escalation What to document, when to escalate, practice activities using anonymised real case structures.
4
What it achieved

89% voluntary engagement. And a community of practice that nobody designed.

Three months after launch, the average learner had voluntarily engaged with over 340 pieces of external content through Degreed. The curation continued after the course ended — learners retained access to their Degreed feed indefinitely, making the learning relationship an ongoing resource rather than a completed event.

The unexpected outcome: Degreed’s social features — bookmarking, commenting, sharing — were not part of the original brief. Within six weeks, staff were using them spontaneously. A practitioner community formed around the content feed. The L&D team formalised this by creating curated role-based pathways from the most-shared, highest-rated content. The AI surfaced the content. The people decided what mattered.

Q
Be ready for pushback

The tough questions. Answer them with confidence.

Interviewers will probe your decisions and test whether you genuinely own this story. Here are the objections you are most likely to face.

Platform & Approach
🤔
Why Degreed specifically? And why not just provide a curated reading list?
▾
Your response

A curated reading list is static — selected once, by a person, using the knowledge available at that moment. Degreed is dynamic — it scans the web continuously and surfaces content based on each learner’s role, engagement history, and the configured topic taxonomy. In a field where a landmark report or a significant crisis development can change practice guidance within days, static curation is structurally inadequate.

The personalisation layer is also critical. A child protection officer in a displacement camp context received different recommendations from a programme manager in a coordination role. A curated reading list treats all staff the same. Degreed treats each learner as an individual with a specific knowledge context.

🤔
How did you ensure the Degreed content was appropriate given the sensitive subject matter?
▾
Your response

The configuration of the AI engine was the primary guardrail. I built a trusted source taxonomy — a curated list of approved domains that the engine was seeded with: UN agency publications, major humanitarian journals, established NGO research outputs, reputable international journalism, and practitioner-led communities. The engine prioritised these sources and flagged content from outside the taxonomy for manual review before it entered the feed.

The second layer was the learner community itself. Within six weeks of launch, staff were flagging and downvoting content that did not meet their professional standards. The social rating system provided a continuous quality filter that supplemented the technical curation guardrails.

Design Philosophy
Why design a course that deliberately points learners away from the course?▾
Because the course is not the point. The performance is the point. If a Degreed article about trafficking routes in conflict contexts is more relevant to a field officer’s current deployment than anything I could build into a Storyline module, pointing them toward it is the best instructional decision I can make. A course that treats itself as the final word on a complex, evolving field is not serving its learners — it is serving its own completeness.
What happens to compliance tracking when learners spend most of their time outside the course?▾
Nothing — because the xAPI integration tracked both environments. Mandatory Storyline completions were logged as usual for compliance purposes. Degreed engagement was tracked separately but visible in the same Learning Record Store. The compliance team had full evidence of mandatory module completion. The L&D team had a much richer picture of actual learning behaviour. The two are not in conflict — they answer different questions.
