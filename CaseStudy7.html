Case Study

From manual to mastery — training 400 users on a new enterprise application

A multi-format eLearning programme combining video explainers, Show Me demonstrations, Try Me practice activities, and scenario-based assessments — designed to take 400 users from never having seen the system to confident and certified before go-live.

The challenge

A system go-live date that would not move — and 400 users who had never seen the application.

The organisation had just completed a major ERP implementation — a new platform replacing three legacy systems at once. The go-live date was not moving. And the training programme, which should have started six weeks earlier, was still unbuilt.

Speed was not the only problem. Finance teams, operations managers, and front-line processing staff all had distinct workflows within the same system. A single generic programme would not work for any of them. Six bespoke role-based pathways had to be designed, built, and deployed in under four months.

  • No existing training materials — the system had been implemented by a third-party vendor whose documentation was technical, not instructional
  • Six distinct user roles, each requiring a separate learning pathway with role-relevant workflows and scenarios
  • Users were geographically dispersed across four office locations and two countries — no single classroom solution was viable
  • The application interface was still being finalised during early development, requiring an iterative build approach as screens changed
  • Assessment completion and pass rates had to be reported to senior leadership as evidence of readiness before go-live approval
Taking stock

Before a single screen was built, we conducted a full content and capability audit — mapping what existed, what was missing, and what each user role actually needed to be able to do on day one.

📋
Vendor Documentation Review
Reviewed 340 pages of technical system documentation. Identified the 12% that was instructionally usable. The rest was configuration and IT reference material.
🎯
Role & Task Analysis
Ran structured interviews with 18 SMEs across six user groups. Mapped every task each role would perform in the system, frequency of use, and consequence of error.
🗺️
Workflow Mapping
Produced six end-to-end workflow maps — one per user role — covering every screen, input field, and decision point a user would encounter in their day-to-day operation of the system.
⚡
Priority Triage
Applied a must-know / need-to-know / nice-to-know framework to every identified task. Only must-know and need-to-know tasks made it into the training programme. This cut scope by 38%.
💡
The audit finding that changed everything
The SME interviews surfaced something important: the errors users were most likely to make were not about understanding the system. They were about not understanding the underlying business process the system was designed to support. That shifted the entire design. Teach the process first. Then the system. Every video, every Show Me, every Try Me was sequenced around that.
The approach

A four-layer architecture applied consistently across all six role pathways — explain, demonstrate, practise, assess — each layer building directly on the one before.

📹 Video Explainers

Produced videos with voiceover and animation

Each module opened with a short produced explainer — typically three to five minutes — that established the business context before introducing the system. These were not screen recordings. They were purpose-built animations with professional voiceover, combining process diagrams, annotated interface previews, and scenario narration.

  • 42 videos produced across the six pathways, averaging 3.8 minutes each
  • Each video introduced the business process, then connected it to what the user would see in the system
  • Deliberately non-technical in tone — designed to be watched, not studied
  • Accessible with closed captions and script transcripts for all users
👁️ Show Me

Guided demonstrations of every key workflow

Following each video, users were taken into a fully interactive Show Me demonstration — a step-by-step guided walkthrough of the actual system interface, with hotspots, callout annotations, and audio commentary at each stage. Users watched and read; they did not yet interact.

  • Built in Articulate Storyline using captured screen recordings overlaid with interactive layers
  • Every click, field entry, and navigation step annotated with purpose — not just instruction
  • Pause points built in at decision nodes where users commonly make errors
  • Branching commentary for role variants — finance users saw finance-specific annotations within shared workflows
🖱️ Try Me

Guided practice with real system simulation

The Try Me activity placed users inside a pixel-accurate simulation of the application interface — built using captured screens — and asked them to complete real tasks independently. The simulation provided contextual hints when users stalled, and tracked errors without penalising exploration.

  • Three levels of support: full hints available, hints on request, no hints (mastery mode)
  • Errors triggered contextual feedback — not generic "incorrect" messages, but specific guidance on what went wrong and why
  • Each Try Me mapped directly to the preceding Show Me — same workflow, same screens, no surprises
  • Completion of Try Me in mastery mode unlocked a readiness badge per module
✅ Assessment

Scenario-based assessment with structured reporting

Each pathway concluded with a summative assessment — not a knowledge quiz, but a scenario-based evaluation in which users were presented with realistic work situations and asked to make the right decision within the system. Results fed into a live dashboard visible to learning administrators and line managers.

  • Scenario questions drawn directly from the most common real-world errors identified in the SME interviews
  • 80% pass threshold required; users who failed received targeted remediation pointing to specific modules, not the whole programme
  • Results reported by role, location, and team — giving managers visibility of individual and group readiness
  • Completion certificates auto-generated and logged in the LMS as evidence of pre-go-live readiness
The performance support layer

A structured FAQ library built for the moment users get stuck

Alongside the formal learning modules, the project included a structured FAQ library — a searchable, role-filtered reference resource designed for use after training, at the point of need. This was not an afterthought. It was a deliberate decision made during the content audit, when SMEs were asked not just what users needed to learn, but what they would need to look up six weeks after go-live.

The FAQ library was built from a structured question-gathering process — SMEs were asked to anticipate every question a user might ask in their first 90 days, grouped by workflow, role, and frequency. Over 220 questions were catalogued. Each was answered concisely, with a link to the relevant module for users who needed a deeper refresher.

How we gathered the questions

Rather than writing FAQs in isolation, we ran structured Q&A simulation workshops with SMEs — asking them to roleplay as confused users asking questions after go-live. This produced far richer, more realistic questions than a straightforward document review would have generated.

The challenges

What made this project genuinely difficult — and how we resolved it.

01
A moving target: the system kept changing
Screen captures taken during early development became obsolete as the vendor made interface changes right up to four weeks before go-live. Show Me demonstrations and Try Me simulations built against earlier builds had to be rebuilt from scratch in some cases.
How we resolved itWe introduced a screen freeze policy with the client — no interface changes after week ten without formal impact assessment. We also built a rapid-rebuild workflow that could update a Show Me within 48 hours of a confirmed screen change.
02
SME availability: knowledge locked inside busy people
The subject matter experts who held all the knowledge were also the people implementing the system. Their time was fractured across the project, and long review cycles threatened to compress the build phase into an unworkable window.
How we resolved itWe replaced sequential review with a targeted sign-off model: each SME was assigned to specific modules only, with a 48-hour turnaround expectation and a structured review template that focused their attention on factual accuracy, not editorial preference.
03
Role overlap: shared screens, different purposes
Many screens in the system were shared across multiple user roles, but used differently by each. Building separate Show Me sequences for every role would have tripled build time. Building a single generic version would have confused users who encountered fields and options irrelevant to their role.
How we resolved itWe introduced layered branching within shared modules — a single core Show Me with role-specific annotation layers that activated based on the user's pathway. Users saw the same interface walkthrough, but with commentary, callouts, and focus areas tailored to their role.
04
Engagement: making system training feel relevant
Application training has a reputation problem. Users approach it as a box-ticking obligation rather than genuine preparation. Early pilot feedback confirmed this risk — participants were clicking through Show Me sequences without reading the annotations.
How we resolved itWe embedded consequence moments into every module — brief scenarios showing what happens when a common error is made in the real system. These were placed before the Show Me, not after. Users who understood the cost of getting it wrong paid far more attention to the demonstration of how to get it right.
05
Video production at volume and speed
Forty-two produced videos in four months — alongside the eLearning build itself — was an aggressive production schedule. Script approvals, voiceover recording, animation, and client review cycles all had to run in parallel without compressing quality.
How we resolved itWe established a modular video production system — a library of reusable animation components, a pre-approved script template that reduced review cycles to a single round, and a dedicated voiceover artist on retainer who could turn around recordings within 24 hours of script sign-off.
06
Assessment integrity: preventing box-ticking
Early assessment designs were flagged by the client as too easy — users could pass by eliminating obviously wrong options without genuinely understanding the system. The assessment had to function as a real measure of readiness, not a completion certificate dressed up as a test.
How we resolved itAll knowledge-recall questions were replaced with scenario-based situational questions that required users to apply understanding, not retrieve information. Distractors were written to be plausible — close-but-wrong options that would catch users who had a vague rather than solid understanding of the workflow.
The design insight
What made this work

Process before system — always

The single most important design decision was sequencing. Every module taught the underlying business process before introducing the system interface. Users who understood why a workflow existed navigated the how far more confidently than users who were simply shown which buttons to press. The videos carried the process narrative. The Show Me and Try Me carried the system navigation. Neither worked without the other.

The unexpected lesson

The FAQ library became the most used asset post go-live

In the three months following go-live, the structured FAQ library recorded more user interactions than any individual eLearning module. Users who had completed their training returned to it repeatedly when they encountered real tasks for the first time under production conditions. This validated the decision to invest in performance support as a formal deliverable rather than an informal afterthought — and it is now a standard component of every application training programme we design.

The outcome

Four hundred users. One go-live date. Zero production incidents in the first thirty days.

Every user completed their role-based pathway and cleared the summative assessment before the go-live date. The project was the first major system rollout in the organisation's recent history to achieve 100% pre-go-live training completion across all user groups.

In the 30 days following go-live, the support desk logged 62% fewer system-related queries than equivalent rollouts. Users attributed this directly to the Try Me practice activities — having completed tasks in the simulation meant the real system felt familiar, not foreign, on day one.
