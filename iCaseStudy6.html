Case Study 6 — Six Wards. Zero Room for Error.

A gamified IPC assessment that turned a routine compliance test into a high-stakes clinical ward round — where every wrong decision costs Care Credits, and a real patient’s discharge depends on getting it right.

1
Where the story begins

A compliance test that nobody took seriously. And why that was a patient safety problem.

Infection Prevention and Control assessment is mandatory in healthcare settings. It is also, in most organisations, treated as an administrative obligation rather than a genuine test of clinical readiness. Staff click through, select the obviously correct answers, pass, and return to the ward. The completion data looks excellent. The behaviour change does not follow.

The client was a healthcare organisation that had identified a gap between IPC assessment performance and actual IPC compliance on the ward. Staff who passed the test were still making the errors the test was designed to prevent. The problem was not knowledge — it was the absence of consequence in the assessment environment. When nothing matters in a test, nothing changes in practice.

The design briefCreate an IPC assessment that feels real — where the decisions carry weight, the errors have visible consequences, and the experience is close enough to clinical reality that it changes how staff think and act on the ward. Do not make it feel like a test. Make it feel like a ward round gone wrong.
🧠 Remember This

"The insight was simple: if you want someone to behave differently in high-stakes reality, give them a high-stakes experience in a safe environment. The gamification was not the point. The consequence was the point."

2
The game mechanic

Care Credits. Lose them and the patient doesn’t go home.

The assessment placed each learner inside six consecutive clinical ward environments. In each ward, they encountered a series of IPC-relevant situations — a patient in isolation, a medication round, a procedure preparation, a visitor management scenario. Every correct IPC decision maintained or earned Care Credits. Every incorrect decision cost them.

The Care Credits mechanic was the key design decision. It created cumulative consequence — each decision affected not just the immediate score but the capacity to succeed in subsequent wards. A learner who depleted their Credits in Ward 1 found Ward 6 much harder. This mirrored clinical reality: IPC failures compound. One missed hand hygiene step does not cause an infection. A pattern of them does.

1
Ward 1 — Hand Hygiene The five moments of hand hygiene in a busy bay setting — where the correct moment is not always obvious.
2
Ward 2 — PPE Selection Choosing the correct PPE for different clinical contact types — under time pressure.
3
Ward 3 — Isolation Procedures Managing a patient in contact isolation — who to tell, how to communicate, what the room protocol requires.
4
Ward 4 — Waste Management Identifying and segregating clinical waste correctly — with plausible wrong options that look reasonable.
5
Ward 5 — Outbreak Response Responding to a suspected norovirus cluster — priority actions, escalation, and communication.
6
Ward 6 — The Final Ward All IPC competencies tested in combination — a complex clinical scenario where every prior decision influences the options available.
⚠️
Challenge: making wrong answers plausible without being misleading

Healthcare assessment has a specific risk: if the wrong answers are obviously wrong, the test is trivial. But if the wrong answers could be genuinely mistaken for correct clinical practice, the assessment itself becomes a source of misinformation.

✅
Resolution

Every distractor was reviewed by an IPC clinical specialist to confirm three things: it was a real error that real staff make, it would not reinforce incorrect practice if a learner selected it and read the feedback, and the feedback explanation clearly distinguished it from the correct action. The clinical review was not optional — it was built into the production timeline as a non-negotiable quality gate.

3
The technical build

Storyline 360. Variables, triggers, and a consequence engine built from the ground up.

The programme was built in Articulate Storyline 360 using a Care Credits variable that tracked across all six wards. Every incorrect response triggered a Credit deduction via a trigger sequence — and the Credit total was visible throughout, updating in real time as a persistent on-screen counter. Learners could not ignore their Credit balance; it was always visible.

The six ward environments were built as photographic backdrops with animated patient and staff characters — creating enough visual realism to engage the clinical imagination without requiring full video production. The scenarios were written to be genuinely ambiguous at the moment of decision — plausible enough that the correct answer required real IPC knowledge, not test-taking strategy.

The 91% perfect score rateThis statistic is counterintuitive — high scores in a gamified assessment could suggest the test was too easy. In this case, the 91% rate followed a redesign of the assessment from a previous version that produced only 58% compliance-level scores. The improvement reflected not easier questions but more effective pre-assessment preparation — the gamification itself was preceded by a short IPC refresher module that primed the relevant knowledge immediately before the assessment.
4
What it achieved

91% perfect scores. And a completion culture that had never existed before.

The previous IPC assessment had a completion problem as well as a performance problem. Compliance-driven completion was high — but ‘completion’ often meant opening the assessment, clicking through, and closing it. The gamified version produced a different pattern: learners who started the ward round completed it, because the Care Credits mechanic created genuine investment in the outcome.

Q
Be ready for pushback

The tough questions. Answer them with confidence.

Interviewers will probe your decisions and test whether you genuinely own this story. Here are the objections you are most likely to face.

Approach & Methodology
🤔
Why gamification for a clinical compliance assessment? Isn’t this a serious topic that deserves a serious format?
▾
Your response

The question contains a false premise: that seriousness requires a serious format. The most serious patient safety topics deserve the most effective learning interventions — not the most earnest-looking ones. The previous assessment was very serious in appearance and produced a 58% compliance-level performance rate. The gamified version — which treated the learner as a capable clinical professional navigating real consequences — produced a 91% perfect score rate.

The gamification was not trivialisation. The Care Credits mechanic was a patient safety consequence in game form. Learners who lost Credits lost the capacity to help a patient go home. The emotional weight of that consequence was far greater than the emotional weight of a red screen saying ‘incorrect.’

🤔
How do you ensure the gamified experience transfers to real ward behaviour?
▾
Your response

The transfer mechanism is the specificity of the scenarios. Every ward situation was drawn from actual IPC failure patterns documented in clinical incident reports — not invented situations, but reconstructed versions of real scenarios that had caused real problems. When a learner encounters a situation on the assessment that mirrors something they have seen on the ward, the learning is not ‘this is what IPC compliance looks like’ — it is ‘I have been in this situation before and now I know I responded incorrectly.’ That recognition is the transfer trigger.

Design Decisions
How did you set the Care Credits value and deduction rate?▾
The Credit values were calibrated to create genuine jeopardy without making the assessment unpassable. An IPC clinical specialist and I worked through every scenario and assigned Credits based on the clinical severity of the error — a missed hand hygiene moment cost fewer Credits than an incorrect isolation decision. The total Credit budget was set so that a learner who made three significant errors would reach the final ward in a genuinely compromised position — requiring them to make correct decisions on the most complex scenarios to avoid failing.
What happened when a learner ran out of Care Credits?▾
They did not fail immediately. The assessment continued — but with a visible indicator that the patient’s discharge was at risk. The final scenario in Ward 6 was designed so that a learner who had depleted their Credits faced a more difficult version of the final decision. Failing to pass Ward 6 triggered a targeted remediation pathway — specific to the errors made — rather than a requirement to repeat the entire assessment. Targeted remediation based on actual errors is far more effective than generic repetition.
