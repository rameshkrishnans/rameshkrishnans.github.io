Case Study

Beyond the course — AI-curated learning that kept field staff connected to a changing world

An eLearning programme for frontline humanitarian workers that used Degreed LXP to serve live, AI-curated articles, reports, and videos from across the internet — keeping knowledge current long after the final module was complete.

The challenge

Frontline staff working in some of the world’s most difficult situations — trained on content that was already out of date by the time they completed it.

The organisation deployed frontline staff across active conflict zones, refugee displacement corridors, and communities affected by exploitation and trafficking. Their work required not just foundational knowledge — safeguarding, trauma-informed practice, child protection — but a real-time understanding of a field that never stood still.

The existing eLearning was a set of fixed modules, updated annually at best. By the time a new hire completed them, significant reports had been published, practice guidance had changed, and the crisis situations referenced in the training had moved on. The course was always, to some degree, already behind.

The organisation needed something that held structured foundational learning alongside a live, constantly updated window into the field as it actually existed.

  • Staff were based across 28 countries with inconsistent internet connectivity — any solution had to function on low-bandwidth connections and mobile devices
  • The subject matter was sensitive — not all internet content on conflict, trafficking, or exploitation was appropriate or safe to serve to field staff without curation guardrails
  • Mandatory module completions had to remain trackable through the LMS for compliance — the external content layer could not replace this requirement
  • The L&D team had two people — manual curation of hundreds of external resources was not viable; automation was the only workable approach
  • Learners were experienced professionals who would disengage immediately from content that felt generic or irrelevant to their specific role or region
The approach

A two-layer approach: structured core learning in Storyline, live curated content through Degreed — linked by xAPI so every external interaction fed back into a single learner record.

🌐
AI-Curated Content Feed
Degreed’s AI engine scanned the web continuously — surfacing articles, NGO reports, video talks, and podcasts based on each learner’s role, region, and topic profile. Updated daily, not annually.
👤
Personalised Learning Feed
Each learner’s feed was calibrated to their function. Child protection officers received different recommendations from supply chain staff — even when working within the same programme and country.
🔗
Storyline Integration via xAPI
Core modules were built in Articulate Storyline 360 and tracked via xAPI to a Learning Record Store — capturing both mandatory completions and voluntary Degreed engagement in a unified learner record.
📦
Embedded Content via Web Objects
Selected Degreed content was embedded directly inside Storyline modules using web objects — placing live curated resources at the exact point in the course where they added most instructional value.
💡
Why Degreed LXP — and not a content library subscription
A curated content library would have provided a fixed catalogue — updated periodically, selected by a publisher, and structured around the publisher’s taxonomy rather than each learner’s context. Degreed pulls from the open web: academic publications, NGO reports, UN documentation, international journalism, and practitioner communities. In a domain where a landmark report can change practice guidance within days, that distinction between a static catalogue and a live, AI-driven feed was the decisive factor.
The content design

Six core modules that taught the foundations — and opened the door to everything else.

The Storyline modules were deliberately scoped to do less than a conventional eLearning programme would attempt. Rather than trying to cover every aspect of a complex, evolving subject domain, each module focused on the foundational frameworks a staff member could not function without — then directed learners outward into Degreed to explore further.

This was a philosophy as much as a design decision. The course stopped trying to be the complete source of knowledge. It became the starting point for engaging with a much richer world of knowledge beyond it.

How Degreed content appeared inside Storyline

At the end of each module, a curated Degreed content feed was embedded directly via web object — showing three to five live-updated resources related to that module’s topic. Learners could engage with content from within the course, with interactions tracked via xAPI. The feed updated automatically as Degreed surfaced new material, making re-visits to the course genuinely worthwhile.

📚
Module 1 — Understanding the Context
The global landscape of conflict, displacement, and exploitation — the scale, the patterns, and the systemic drivers. Designed to ground all subsequent learning in field reality, not an abstracted version of it.
🛡️
Module 2 — Safeguarding Principles & Policy
Core safeguarding frameworks, mandatory reporting obligations, and organisational policy — with branched scenarios placing staff inside situations where they had to apply the frameworks under genuine pressure.
🧠
Module 3 — Trauma-Informed Practice
How trauma shapes behaviour and communication — and how staff can engage with affected individuals in ways that support recovery rather than inadvertently causing further harm.
🤝
Module 4 — Community Engagement & Cultural Competence
Working respectfully within communities that are not your own — navigating cultural difference, building trust with local partners, and avoiding the imposition of external assumptions onto local realities.
⚠️
Module 5 — Staff Safety & Personal Resilience
Security protocols, field risk assessment, and the psychological demands of high-trauma environments — including vicarious trauma, its signs, and how to support yourself and colleagues who are carrying it.
📋
Module 6 — Reporting, Documentation & Escalation
What to document, how to document it, when to escalate, and who to escalate to — with practice activities using anonymised real case structures that brought the procedures to life.
Curation in action

What the AI surfaced — and how it kept learning alive long after the course ended.

The Degreed AI engine was configured with a topic taxonomy built from the six module themes — seeded with trusted source domains including UN agency publications, major humanitarian journals, established NGO research outputs, reputable international journalism, and practitioner-led communities.

Each learner received a personalised weekly digest of newly surfaced content — typically five to eight items — calibrated to their role and the topics they had engaged with most. A child protection officer working in a displacement setting received markedly different recommendations from a programme manager in a coordination role, even within the same organisation.

Crucially, the curation did not stop when the course did. Learners retained access to their Degreed feed indefinitely — meaning the learning relationship with the organisation continued as a live, growing resource rather than ending at module completion.

The design insight
What made this work

The course that pointed outward — not inward

Most eLearning is designed to be self-contained. It tries to capture everything the learner needs inside its own walls. This programme did the opposite. Every module ended by explicitly naming what it could not cover — and directing learners outward into Degreed to find it.

This created something unusual: a course that grew the learner’s appetite for knowledge rather than satisfying it. Learners who finished the modules did not feel like they had completed something. They felt like they had started something. The 89% voluntary engagement rate with the Degreed feed was the direct result of this design choice.

The moment the design clicked During a pilot session, a senior field officer said: “This is the first training I have done in years that treated me as someone who wanted to keep learning — not someone who needed to be forced through a module.” That single observation shaped the final design more than any data point.
The unexpected outcome

Learners started sharing — and the feed became a community resource

Degreed’s social features — the ability to bookmark, comment on, and share content with colleagues — were not part of the original brief. They were included as default platform functionality and initially left unconfigured.

Within six weeks of launch, staff were using them spontaneously. Field officers were bookmarking reports and sharing them with their teams. A practitioner community was forming around the content feed that had not been designed or facilitated — it had emerged from giving knowledgeable people access to good content and a way to discuss it.

The L&D team formalised this by creating curated Degreed pathways for specific roles — collections of the most-shared, highest-rated content assembled by the community itself. The AI surfaced the content. The people decided what mattered.

The outcome

Staff who finished the course and kept on learning — because the world they worked in never stopped changing.

The programme met its compliance requirements — all mandatory modules completed and tracked. But the measure that mattered most was continued engagement. In a field where knowledge becomes outdated faster than any course can be updated, keeping staff connected to a live, evolving body of knowledge was the actual learning outcome the organisation needed.

Three months after launch, the average learner had voluntarily engaged with over 340 pieces of external content through Degreed — reading, watching, bookmarking, and sharing material that had nothing to do with their mandatory requirements and everything to do with doing their job better in a world that had not stood still.

“I have done a lot of mandatory training over the years. This is the first time I've gone back to it after completing it — not because I had to, but because there was always something new worth reading.”

— Senior Field Programme Officer
