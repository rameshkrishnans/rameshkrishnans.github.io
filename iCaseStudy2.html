Case Study 2 — The Clinical Simulation

A nine-stage business simulation with gamification that trained Clinical Research Associates to manage hospital monitoring visits — built from a two-page brief with no existing source material.

1
Where the story begins

A two-page brief. No manuals. No guides. No existing training.

The client was a leading Contract Research Organisation. Their new Clinical Research Associates — the people responsible for monitoring live pharmaceutical clinical trials in hospitals — were being deployed to trial sites with no structured preparation. Onboarding was informal, inconsistent, and left entirely to the line manager's discretion. There was no training programme, no defined learning path, and no mechanism to verify readiness before a new CRA stood in a hospital corridor with a patient record in their hands.

When I asked for the source material, the client handed me a two-page document listing nine workflow steps with brief descriptions. That was everything. There were no manuals, no reference guides, no process documentation, and no existing training materials of any kind. All knowledge resided with experienced CRAs and their managers.

The core challenge in one sentenceBuild a nine-stage simulation of the complete CRA monitoring workflow — to a clinical accuracy standard that could withstand regulatory scrutiny — from a two-page brief, with a four-month deadline.
🧠 Remember This

"The brief was two pages. The simulation was nine stages. Everything in between was extracted from people’s heads through structured conversation — and shaped into content that could survive a regulatory audit."

2
Understanding the workflow

Structured SME sessions. Six weeks of extraction before a single screen was built.

With no documents to draw from, the entire curriculum had to be built from expert knowledge. I ran structured SME extraction sessions with experienced CRAs and clinical operations managers — working through each of the nine workflow stages in sequence, asking not just ‘what do you do’ but ‘what do you decide, and what happens if you decide wrong.’

The decision-consequence mapping was critical. CRA errors in the field carry direct regulatory and patient safety implications. The simulation had to teach not just the steps, but the stakes. That required understanding not just what the right action was, but what the realistic wrong actions were — and why they were wrong.

⚠️
Challenge: time zone and availability

The development team operated in a different time zone from the client, and SMEs were field staff with unpredictable schedules. Review cycles were at constant risk of delay.

✅
Resolution

I introduced an asynchronous review protocol — structured review templates with yes/no accuracy gates and a 48-hour response expectation. SMEs reviewed specific stages, not the whole programme. This kept the project moving without requiring real-time availability.

3
The instructional strategy

Simulation designed around real workflow — not theory.

The central design decision was to build the programme as a simulation rather than an informational eLearning. Each stage recreated the actual decisions, documents, and interactions a CRA would encounter in the field — from scheduling hospital visits to reconciling patient data in the eCRF system.

The gamification system reinforced progression. Learners earned reward points within each stage, with summative assessment gates that had to be cleared before the next stage unlocked. Each stage was a separate SCORM package, enabling flexible deployment and granular tracking. The big-picture workflow was presented at the outset and revisited at regular intervals — reinforcing the mental model of the full process even as learners worked through individual stages.

The early prototype that changed the projectA working prototype of Stage 1 was built from the two-page brief alone — before full content had been agreed — to give the client something concrete to react to. The response was immediate and positive. It unlocked deeper collaboration and a steady flow of expert knowledge that fuelled the full programme build.
"For the first time, our new CRAs arrive at sites already knowing the process. The simulation made it real for them before they’d even taken their first visit." — Head of Clinical Operations

Q
Be ready for pushback

The tough questions. Answer them with confidence.

Interviewers will probe your decisions and test whether you genuinely own this story. Here are the objections you are most likely to face.

Approach & Design
🤔
Why a simulation instead of a standard eLearning with scenarios?
▾
Your response

Standard eLearning teaches information, then tests recall. A simulation places the learner inside the workflow — making decisions, experiencing consequences, and building the procedural memory that a CRA needs to operate under regulatory pressure in a real hospital. The CRA monitoring visit is not a knowledge task. It is a performance task. The only way to prepare for a performance task is to practise it. The simulation was the practise environment.

The additional design advantage: because each stage mirrored a real step in the workflow, experienced CRAs could use the programme as a structured refresher — returning to specific stages rather than repeating the whole programme. A standard eLearning course cannot offer that level of targeted remediation.

🤔
You had a two-page brief and a four-month deadline. How do you defend that as sufficient discovery time?
▾
Your response

The two pages were the starting point for discovery, not the source material for the programme. The entire curriculum was extracted through structured SME sessions over six weeks — those sessions produced everything the two pages could not contain: the decisions within each step, the common errors, the regulatory stakes, the real-world context. The brief told me what stages existed. The SME sessions told me what actually happened inside them.

Instructional Design
How did you handle the regulatory accuracy requirement?▾
Every piece of clinical content — every simulation scenario, every document displayed in the eCRF or patient records stages — was reviewed for accuracy by clinical SMEs before inclusion. Where clinical accuracy was non-negotiable, I built in formal sign-off checkpoints rather than relying on informal review. The assessment pass threshold was set at 95% precisely because the regulatory stakes required a high bar.
What was the gamification rationale?▾
The gamification was not decoration — it was a structural mechanism. Stage-based reward points created a sense of achievement at every step, not just at programme completion. The assessment gate mechanism meant that progression was earned rather than assumed — which mirrored the real-world reality that a CRA does not move to the next monitoring visit task until the previous one is complete. The gamification design was instructionally grounded, not motivationally bolted on.
