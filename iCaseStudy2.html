1   Where the story begins

Managers being asked to make senior decisions without ever having practised them.

The client was a leading consumer goods organisation expanding rapidly into Southeast Asia. Mid-level managers needed to operate at business unit level — owning P&Ls, negotiating with CFOs, designing routes to market under real pressure.

The problem was that most of them had never done any of those things. They had the potential. They had not had the experience. Classroom training had been tried. It produced knowledge — not capability. The organisation needed managers to actually experience the complexity of senior decision-making, not read about it.

The brief that came to my team was straightforward in intent and daunting in scope: design a learning experience that gives mid-level managers the genuine feel of operating at business unit level — in a way that produces measurable behavioural change, generates data HR can act on, and works within a five-month delivery window.

🧠
Remember This
"The organisation did not need training. It needed a practice environment — a space where managers could make senior decisions, experience real consequences, and develop the capability that only comes from repeated practice under pressure. That framing shaped every design decision that followed."

Five constraints made this harder than a standard commission:

  • ①
    Zero source content
    The client had a competency framework and a list of target behaviours. No case library, no process documentation, no existing training materials of any kind. The entire curriculum had to be built from scratch.
  • ②
    Dual purpose — development and assessment
    The programme had to function as a development experience and as an assessment. HR needed data they could actually use — not just participant satisfaction scores.
  • ③
    Fictionalised but credible business context
    Scenarios had to feel real enough to be challenging, but fictionalised to avoid competitive sensitivity. A fictional market had to carry real commercial weight.
  • ④
    Three-time-zone build team
    The development team was distributed across three time zones. Everything ran on tight asynchronous sprints with no margin for misalignment.
  • ⑤
    Multiple cohort types
    The programme had to serve first-time cohorts and re-deployment groups seeking an advanced-level challenge — without building two separate programmes.
2   Building the content from scratch

Six SME workshops in three weeks. Before a single screen was built.

With no documents to draw from, the entire curriculum had to be extracted from expert knowledge. I ran six intensive SME workshops over three weeks — working through each phase of the simulation in sequence, asking not just what decisions were made, but what the real trade-offs were and what happened when managers got them wrong.

The workshops were structured around decision mapping — not content delivery. For each simulation phase, the key question was: what are the two or three decisions that determine whether this phase succeeds or fails, and what does the realistic wrong decision look like? That framing produced far richer content than asking SMEs to describe best practice.

⚠️
Challenge — time zone and SME availability
SMEs were senior commercial leaders with unpredictable schedules. The build team operated across three time zones. Review cycles were at constant risk of delay, and any slip compressed the remaining build window.
✅
Resolution
I introduced an asynchronous review protocol — structured templates with specific yes/no accuracy gates and a 48-hour response expectation. SMEs reviewed specific phases, not the full programme. This kept the project moving without requiring real-time availability from people who could not reliably provide it.
🧠
Remember This
"A working prototype of Phase 01 was built in week two — before any content was finalised — and shared with the client. That decision turned out to be the most important one of the project. It gave the client something real to react to, sparked the best content conversations of the entire engagement, and dramatically accelerated the SME sessions that followed."
3   The simulation design

Eight phases. Each one a real leadership crucible.

The simulation placed participants inside a single coherent business scenario — taking a new consumer goods brand from market entry analysis through to full performance review. Every phase recreated a real leadership decision with real commercial consequences.

  • 01
    Market Entry Analysis
    Teams analyse a $4.2B market opportunity, assess competitive dynamics, and make an entry recommendation against a 40-minute clock.
    Strategic Thinking
  • 02
    Competitive Intelligence
    Deep-dive into two dominant incumbents using curated market intelligence. Teams must identify exploitable gaps and build a differentiation thesis.
    Analytical Rigour
  • 03
    Financial Modelling
    Build a three-scenario P&L, stress-test assumptions live, then defend the financial model in a Board Challenge with a sceptical CFO.
    Financial Acumen
  • 04
    Stakeholder Negotiation
    Reach alignment with four stakeholders — each with conflicting agendas and specific levers that must be discovered and deployed in real time.
    Influence & Persuasion
  • 05
    Operations Design
    Design the supply chain, distribution architecture, and operating model for the new business unit within fixed cost and lead-time constraints.
    Operational Thinking
  • 06
    Risk & Compliance
    Navigate three live regulatory scenarios — including an unexpected policy change mid-phase — while keeping the launch timeline intact.
    Risk Management
  • 07
    Go-to-Market Launch
    Execute market entry. Allocate budget across channels, respond to a competitive counter-move, and hit Q1 milestones under Board scrutiny.
    Commercial Execution
  • 08
    Performance Review
    Teams present full business unit performance to the Board, connecting every prior decision to the final P&L outcome. Nothing is forgotten.
    Accountability & Reflection
🧠
The critical design decision — decisions compound
"Unlike simulations that reset between modules, this one carries decisions forward. An aggressive market entry in Phase 01 creates tighter margins in Phase 03 and a harder CFO challenge in Phase 07. The compounding consequence architecture was the feature that made the simulation feel real — and the feedback that participants cited most often."
4   The gamification system

Gamification engineered to sustain intensity across 32 hours of programme runtime.

Motivation in a multi-phase programme is not a given. Without deliberate design, engagement peaks early and fades. The gamification system was built to sustain competitive energy, reward the right behaviours, and create the psychological weight of genuine accountability at every stage.

🏆
Live team leaderboard
Four competing teams accumulate points across all eight phases. Rankings update in real time, creating genuine competitive tension that sharpens every decision.
🏅
Achievement badge system
Six unlockable badges reward behaviours beyond correct answers — speed, collaboration, precision, and consistency — ensuring engagement at every level of capability.
🔒
Summative assessment gates
Each phase locks until the assessment is cleared. Progression is earned, not assumed — creating the psychological weight of real-world accountability at every stage.

The gamification design was instructionally grounded, not motivationally bolted on. Each element was tied to a learning outcome: the leaderboard rewarded decision quality under time pressure; the badge system surfaced behaviours that scoring alone could not capture; the assessment gates mirrored the real-world reality that a business unit leader does not move to the next decision until the current one is resolved.

⚠️
Design risk — gamification that undermines learning
Leaderboards can shift focus from learning to winning. There was a risk that teams would game the scoring system rather than engage with the underlying commercial complexity.
✅
Resolution
Points were awarded for decision rationale, not just correct answers. Teams had to justify their choices in structured format before the score was released. This meant that gaming the system required engaging with the content — which was the point.

Q   Be ready for pushback

The tough questions. Answer them with confidence.

Interviewers will probe your decisions and test whether you genuinely own this story. Here are the objections and questions you are most likely to face.

Approach & Design
Why a simulation instead of a standard case-study-based programme or leadership workshop?
▾
Your response

A workshop teaches frameworks. A case study teaches analysis. A simulation teaches decision-making under pressure — which is the actual capability the organisation needed to develop. The managers in this programme were being asked to own P&Ls, negotiate with CFOs, and design go-to-market strategies. None of those are knowledge tasks. They are performance tasks. And the only way to prepare for a performance task is to practise it.

The additional design advantage: a simulation generates data. Decision logs, time-on-task, scoring patterns — these gave HR a granular picture of individual capability that no workshop or case study discussion could produce. That dual function — development and assessment — was built into the brief from the start, and the simulation format was the only one that could deliver both.

You had zero source content and a five-month deadline. How do you defend the front-end investment in discovery?
▾
Your response

The six SME workshops over three weeks were not a luxury — they were a prerequisite. Without them, there was nothing to build. The client had a competency framework and a set of behaviours, not content. Every scenario, every stakeholder persona, every financial model, every decision node had to be extracted from the knowledge of people who had lived these decisions in the real business.

The more important design decision was building a Phase 01 prototype in week two — before the full content was agreed — and putting it in front of the client immediately. That prototype unlocked the best content conversations of the entire project. SMEs could react to something real rather than describe something abstract. It accelerated every review cycle that followed and was the single most effective thing I did to protect the timeline.

How did you ensure the fictionalised business context carried enough credibility to be challenging?
▾
Your response

Credibility in a simulation comes from specificity, not from using real company names. The $4.2B market figure, the two named incumbent competitors, the three-scenario P&L with specific margin targets, the CFO with a documented set of objections — these details made the scenario feel real because they forced the kind of thinking that real commercial decisions require.

The SME workshops were essential here. Every specific number and every stakeholder behaviour in the simulation was grounded in something an experienced commercial leader had actually encountered. The context was fictionalised. The decisions were not. That combination — safe to fail, but realistic enough to learn from — is the design sweet spot for this kind of programme.

Instructional Design
What was the rationale for making decisions compound across phases?
▾
Your response

Real business decisions compound. A market entry choice creates constraints that persist for years. A stakeholder relationship managed poorly in one quarter affects what is possible in the next. If the simulation reset between phases, it would have taught managers to optimise each phase independently — which is precisely the wrong mental model for business unit leadership.

The compounding architecture meant that every decision carried forward. An aggressive entry recommendation in Phase 01 created tighter margins in Phase 03 and a harder CFO challenge in Phase 07. Teams that had not thought strategically early paid for it repeatedly. That cascading consequence structure was the feature that participants cited most consistently as what made the experience feel genuinely challenging — and genuinely educational.

How did you prevent the leaderboard from undermining the learning by turning it into a competition?
▾
Your response

This was a real design risk and one I took seriously. A leaderboard that rewards correct answers alone will produce teams that game the scoring logic rather than engage with the underlying complexity. The design solution was to award points for decision rationale, not just for decisions. Teams had to justify their choices in a structured format before the score was released.

This meant that gaming the system required actually engaging with the content — which was the point. The competitive energy the leaderboard created was real and valuable. The design challenge was ensuring it was directed at the quality of thinking rather than the mechanics of scoring. The badge system reinforced this further — rewarding collaboration, consistency, and speed-under-pressure in addition to correctness, so that the full range of leadership behaviours had explicit recognition in the scoring architecture.

How did the SCORM architecture serve the programme's goals?
▾
Your response

Each phase was packaged as a separate SCORM module within a single coherent programme. That modular architecture served three purposes: it enabled granular LMS tracking at the phase level, so HR could see exactly where individual managers were struggling; it allowed re-deployment groups to return to specific phases for targeted reinforcement without repeating the full programme; and it created natural assessment gates — the LMS could enforce phase-locking based on completion and score thresholds.

The alternative — a single monolithic SCORM package — would have given LMS administrators a completion record and nothing else. The modular approach gave them a capability map. That distinction was important to a client whose stated purpose was to generate data HR could act on, not just programme completion records.

Your Role & Ownership
This is a complex project. What specifically did you personally own versus your team?
▾
Your response

I owned the overall design architecture — the decision to use a simulation format, the eight-phase structure and the competency mapping across phases, the compounding consequence mechanism, and the gamification design rationale. I personally led all six SME workshops, built the Phase 01 prototype that shaped the client relationship, and designed the assessment and scoring framework.

The team's contribution was in content development — building out the Storyline modules for Phases 02 through 08, creating the financial models and stakeholder personas, and managing the logistics of the asynchronous review protocol. My role was design-led and strategy-led. The team executed a design that was fully specified before they began building. The division was deliberate — it kept design intent coherent across eight phases built in parallel.

What would you do differently on a project like this?
▾
Your response

Two things. First, I would build the Phase 01 prototype even earlier — ideally before the SME workshop series rather than during it. The prototype changed the quality of the client conversation so dramatically that I wish it had been available from the very first session. An early tangible artefact is the fastest way to align on direction when you are building from nothing.

Second, I would design a formal transfer measurement plan before delivery rather than after. We captured readiness confidence data and LMS performance data, but we did not have a structured plan for measuring on-the-job behaviour change at 60 and 90 days post-programme. The 97% readiness confidence figure is compelling. A before/after measure of the quality of commercial decisions in the field would have been more compelling still — and would have strengthened the business case for the next phase of the programme.
