Case Study 4 — The HEART Programme

A half-day virtual ILT programme for customer-facing teams that used the HEART framework to turn difficult service interactions into trust-building moments — delivered entirely on Zoom, where passive attention is the path of least resistance.

1
Where the story begins

Good intentions do not make great service interactions.

The client was a large international organisation with significant public-facing operations. Their donor and stakeholder feedback showed a persistent pattern: service interactions were described as technically adequate but emotionally flat. Staff responded promptly and accurately, but often failed to acknowledge the emotional context of the contact. Donors felt processed, not heard. Long-term supporters were quietly disengaging.

The organisation had delivered service awareness content before — policy documents, onboarding modules, a generic soft-skills eLearning — but nothing that changed how people actually behaved in a high-pressure interaction. The training existed. The gap remained.

The brief in fullDesign a half-day virtual programme that goes beyond awareness to build real behavioural fluency — and that works on Zoom, where distraction is constant and passive listening is the default. Every element had to earn its place in the session.
🧠 Remember This

"The client had already tried awareness training. The brief was not for more awareness. It was for behavioural fluency — the ability to apply a framework automatically, under pressure, in the real moment of a difficult call."

2
The instructional framework

HEART. Five letters. Five consistent postures under pressure.

The central tool of the programme — the HEART framework — was designed to be memorable under pressure. Not a script. Not a checklist. A set of five consistent orientations that a service professional brings to every interaction, regardless of what the customer presents.

H
Hear Listen without interruption. Reflect back what you heard before moving to any response. The act of being heard is itself a service act — not a precondition for one.
E
Empathise Acknowledge the emotion first. Name what the customer is feeling before addressing the issue. If they do not feel heard, they cannot receive the solution you are about to offer.
A
Apologise Own the moment. Say sorry for the experience — even if you did not cause it. Ownership in this moment is not an admission of fault. It is the fastest way to restore trust.
R
Resolve Offer a clear, specific path forward. If you can fix it fully, say how and when. If you cannot, say explicitly what you can do. Vague reassurance is worse than none.
T
Thank Treat feedback as a gift. Thank the customer for raising the issue. Every complaint that reaches a human is an opportunity to prevent the far more common alternative: a customer who says nothing and simply leaves.
⚠️
Challenge: making HEART feel like a posture

Early testing revealed participants who memorised the framework applied it rigidly — producing interactions that felt scripted. The framework needed to feel like a natural orientation, not a checklist.

✅
Resolution

The solution was sequencing. HEART was introduced experientially — participants practised the behaviours in pairs before the acronym was revealed. By the time they learned the framework name, they recognised it as a label for something they had already done successfully. That reversal — practise first, name later — made HEART feel like articulation of natural behaviour rather than imposition of a new script.

3
The engagement strategy

Passive attention is the path of least resistance. The design made passivity impossible.

The client had a standing concern about virtual engagement — previous webinars had seen significant drop-off after the first 30 minutes. Every module was designed with a scheduled interaction point within 10 minutes of content delivery.

1
Poll before you teach Module 1 opens with a four-option Zoom poll before any content is delivered. Learners commit to an answer. The spread of responses becomes the lesson.
2
The Chat Waterfall A scenario is read aloud. Participants type their HEART response in chat but do not send. On the facilitator’s cue: ‘Three... two... one... send.’ The chat floods simultaneously. Every voice is visible. Groupthink is eliminated because nobody could see anyone else’s response before committing.
3
Breakout rooms as sense-making Three structured breakout activities — each with a specific task, a timed return, and a facilitated debrief. Tight enough to produce focused conversation. Loose enough to surface genuine differences.
4
Role-play pairs HEART applied under simulated pressure — one participant as service staff, one as a frustrated donor. Debrief focused on what each person noticed, not what they performed correctly.
5
Commitment card One specific behaviour. One accountability partner. Named aloud before the session closed.
The three programme assets delivered1. A 15-slide vILT Slide Deck with facilitator cues, poll triggers, and HEART framework visuals. 2. A Facilitator Guide with full script, timing, Zoom management cues, and debrief questions. 3. A Participant Guide with reflection prompts, de-escalation language cards, and the personal commitment card — designed for use after the session, not just during it.
4
What it achieved

78% applied HEART in a real interaction within four weeks of the session.

Observation data collected four weeks after the session showed that 78% of participants had consciously applied at least one HEART step in a real service interaction they identified as difficult. The accountability partner structure — where participants named their partner aloud before closing — was cited as the element that most influenced post-session follow-through.

The Facilitator Guide was adopted without modification for subsequent cohorts, allowing internal facilitators to run the programme independently. The chat waterfall technique and the poll-before-teach structure have since been applied to other programmes across the organisation’s learning calendar.

Q
Be ready for pushback

The tough questions. Answer them with confidence.

Interviewers will probe your decisions and test whether you genuinely own this story. Here are the objections you are most likely to face.

Platform & Delivery
🤔
Why Zoom ILT rather than self-paced eLearning? eLearning would reach more people at lower cost.
▾
Your response

The specific behaviour this programme targeted — recognising and responding to emotional cues in real-time service interactions — cannot be developed through self-paced eLearning. It requires rehearsal under social pressure, with real-time feedback and the cognitive load of a live interaction. Zoom ILT, when designed properly, can create those conditions. Self-paced eLearning cannot.

The cost argument also requires a fuller accounting: the Facilitator Guide means internal facilitators can run the programme for future cohorts at near-zero additional cost. The initial build investment amortises across every deployment. A well-designed live programme is often more cost-effective over time than repeated eLearning licensing.

🤔
How do you manage the quality of Zoom delivery? What if the facilitator is not skilled?
▾
Your response

This was a central concern in the design. The Facilitator Guide was written specifically so that a facilitator who had not designed the programme could run it with confidence. It includes full scripted narration (not just bullet points), exact Zoom instructions for each poll and breakout activity, anticipated participant responses with suggested facilitator replies, and timing guides for each segment.

The programme was also piloted with an internal facilitator — not the designer — before client sign-off. That pilot identified two sections where the instructions were ambiguous and led to hesitation. Both were revised before the final guide was delivered.

Design Decisions
What was the instructional rationale for the chat waterfall?▾
The chat waterfall solved a fundamental virtual learning problem: in a standard Zoom chat, participants wait to see what others say before committing. The slowest, most considered responses get the most visibility. The most automatic — and therefore most revealing — responses get suppressed. By requiring participants to write their response and hold it until the cue, the waterfall captures first-instinct thinking, which is exactly the type of response that happens in a real service interaction under pressure. It is simultaneously a learning tool and a diagnostic.
How did you adapt the programme for participants who already had extensive service experience?▾
The design premise was that HEART is a posture, not a script — and that experienced practitioners often have the right instincts but inconsistent application. For experienced staff, the role-play debrief shifted from ‘did you apply HEART?’ to ‘where did you instinctively move away from HEART, and why?’ The programme had enough depth in the debrief structure to challenge experienced practitioners without alienating newcomers.
